# Appraisal vs CMA vs PropertyDNA — Which One Do I Trust?

When you are trying to figure out what a home is actually worth, three names keep coming up: the appraisal, the CMA, and — increasingly — PropertyDNA. The short answer is this: an appraisal is the most legally defensible number, a CMA is the most motivated number, and a PropertyDNA report is the most complete picture. Here is the uncomfortable truth: the appraisal does not come until after you are already under contract, the CMA is produced by someone earning a commission on your transaction, and most buyers never see institutional-level comp analysis at all. ## What a CMA Actually Is (and Who It Serves)

A Comparative Market Analysis is a report your real estate agent prepares by pulling recent sales of similar homes — typically within the last 90 days, within a half-mile radius, and within roughly 20 percent of the subject property's square footage. It sounds rigorous. It is not always neutral. Your listing agent uses a CMA to justify a listing price. A buyer's agent uses a CMA to justify an offer price. Both agents earn a percentage of the final sale — nationally that has hovered around 5 to 6 percent combined, though the 2024 NAR settlement has begun reshaping commission structures. The incentive to close at a high number is baked into the model. CMAs also rely entirely on which comps the agent chooses to include. A three-bedroom sold in a flood zone two blocks away is technically a comp. Whether it makes it into your report is a judgment call — one made by someone who gets paid when you sign.

> **A CMA is only as unbiased as the agent who built it. And your agent gets paid when you close, not when you walk away from a bad deal.**

## What a Formal Appraisal Tells You — and When It Arrives

A licensed appraiser produces an independent, standardized valuation that your lender requires before funding a mortgage. The appraiser physically inspects the property, reviews public records, selects their own comps, and issues a report that typically costs between $400 and $700 for a single-family home, sometimes more in complex markets. The appraisal is genuinely independent in ways a CMA is not — appraisers are legally prohibited from having a financial interest in the transaction. So why is it not the end of the story? Because it arrives after you are under contract. By the time you see an appraisal number, you have already negotiated your price, put down earnest money — often 1 to 3 percent of the purchase price — and set your entire budget around a figure you have not yet verified. If the appraisal comes in low, you renegotiate or you walk. But many buyers feel too committed at that stage to walk, and sellers know it.

- Appraisals are backward-looking: they rely on closed sales data, which can be 60 to 120 days old in a fast-moving market.
- Appraisers are generalists covering large geographic areas — they may visit your target neighborhood once a month.
- An appraisal tells you what the lender needs to know, not necessarily what you need to know as a buyer.
- Appraisal gaps — where the appraised value falls below the contract price — affected roughly 7 to 8 percent of transactions during peak competitive markets.
- Appraisals do not assess risk factors: flood zone status, permit history, structural red flags, or neighborhood trend data.

## The Comp Selection Problem: Why the Same Address Gets Three Different Values

One of the most disorienting experiences in a home search is receiving wildly different valuations for the same property. A seller's CMA says $620,000. Your buyer's agent CMA says $595,000. The appraisal comes back at $580,000. All three are looking at the same home. The reason is comp selection — each analyst chose different sold properties as benchmarks. A comp three streets away in an HOA-governed community is technically similar in square footage but meaningfully different in desirability. A comp from 110 days ago predates a local school rezoning that changed buyer demand. None of these factors are automatically accounted for. Automated Valuation Models like Zillow's Zestimate have documented median error rates of around 2 to 3 percent for on-market homes and can exceed 6 to 7 percent for off-market properties — which sounds small until you realize that on a $600,000 home, 6 percent is $36,000. PropertyDNA's comp engine uses institutional-grade filtering to weight recency, micro-location, and structural similarity together, not independently.
