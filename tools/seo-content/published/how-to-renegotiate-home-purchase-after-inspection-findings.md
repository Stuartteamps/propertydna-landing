# How to Renegotiate a Home Purchase After Inspection Findings

After a home inspection reveals problems, you have real leverage — and a legal window to use it. In most contracts, the inspection contingency period (typically 7–14 days) gives you the right to renegotiate the purchase price, request a repair credit, demand specific fixes, or walk away entirely with your earnest money back. The key is acting quickly, staying unemotional, and knowing exactly which findings carry the most financial weight.

Most buyers make one of two mistakes: they either panic and ask for everything on the report, or they feel guilty and ask for nothing. Neither works. Sellers don't owe you a perfect house — but they do owe you an honest price. That gap between what you agreed to pay and what the house actually costs to own is exactly where your renegotiation lives.

## What the Inspection Contingency Actually Gives You

The inspection contingency is one of the few buyer protections written directly into most purchase contracts. It grants you a defined period — commonly 10 days in California, 7–15 days in Florida — to review inspection results and formally object. If you identify material defects during that window, you can submit a written repair request or amendment. If the seller refuses and you cannot reach agreement, you can terminate the contract and recover your earnest deposit, which often runs between 1% and 3% of the purchase price. After that contingency window closes, your leverage shrinks dramatically, so timing is everything.

## How to Triage Inspection Findings: What's Worth Fighting For

Not every line in a 40-page inspection report is a negotiating point. Inspectors flag cosmetic wear all the time — cracked caulk, aging paint, minor trim damage — and asking a seller to address those is a fast way to blow goodwill and poison the deal. Focus your energy on three categories of findings that represent real money.

- Safety hazards: faulty wiring (especially aluminum branch wiring or double-tapped breakers), gas leaks, structural cracks, or active mold. These are non-negotiable items and carry significant remediation costs — mold remediation alone can run $3,000–$30,000 depending on scope.
- Major systems: HVAC units at end of life (typically 15–20 years), water heaters over 10 years old, failing roofs with less than 5 years of life remaining. A full HVAC replacement averages $8,000–$15,000 — a number any seller needs to hear.
- Hidden defects: water intrusion, foundation movement, sewer line issues, or anything the seller may have known about and failed to disclose. These carry legal weight beyond just repair cost.

> **Your inspection report is not a wish list. It is a financial document. Treat it like one.**

## Three Ways to Renegotiate — and When to Use Each

There is no single right way to renegotiate after an inspection. Your strategy should depend on the local market, how motivated the seller is, and how much you want the property. Here are the three main levers buyers use, and the honest trade-offs of each.

1. Request a price reduction: You ask the seller to lower the purchase price to reflect the cost of defects. This is clean, simple, and gives you cash flexibility at closing. Downside: it reduces your loan basis, which matters if you are stretching to qualify.
2. Request a repair credit at closing: Instead of a lower price, the seller gives you a credit — typically applied to closing costs — that you use to fund repairs yourself. This keeps the purchase price intact for financing purposes and lets you choose your own contractors. This is often the most buyer-friendly option.
3. Demand specific repairs before closing: You require the seller to fix identified issues prior to settlement. Use this sparingly — seller-managed repairs are often done on the cheap, and you lose control of quality. Reserve it for clear safety hazards or permit-required work.

## How to Write a Repair Request That Actually Gets Results

Your repair request needs to be specific, documented, and unemotional. Attach relevant pages from the inspection report. Get at least one contractor quote — ideally two — so your dollar figure is grounded in reality rather than guesswork. Sellers and their agents know when a buyer is fishing. If you present a $12,400 electrical remediation quote from a licensed electrician, that number is very hard to argue with. A vague request to 'fix the electrical issues' gives the seller room to do the minimum and call it done. Lead with the safety issues and the big-ticket items. Keep the cosmetic stuff off the list entirely. Your agent should submit the request formally in writing through the contract amendment process — verbal agreements about repairs are worth nothing.

## What to Do When the Seller Says No

Sellers reject repair requests more often than buyers expect, especially in competitive markets. If the seller comes back with a counteroffer that doesn't meet your needs, you have exactly two real choices: accept the gap and proceed, or exercise your inspection contingency and walk. Do not let your agent pressure you into waiving significant repairs to 'save the deal' unless you have done the math on what those repairs will actually cost you out of pocket. A $500,000 house with a $25,000 foundation problem is a $525,000 house. If you are walking, do it in writing before your contingency expires. Missing that deadline means you could lose your earnest deposit even if you have legitimate grounds to leave. Use your contingency window like the legal right it is — because it expires.

## Why Your Agent's Incentives May Not Align With Yours Here

Here is an uncomfortable truth about inspection renegotiations: your buyer's agent gets paid when the deal closes, not when you get the best outcome. A delayed deal or a terminated contract costs them time and commission. That does not mean your agent is dishonest — most are not — but it does mean you should not rely solely on their enthusiasm to know when to push harder or walk away. Independently verify the cost of major repairs. Pull permit records on the property yourself; unpermitted work adds risk and future liability that rarely shows up in an inspection report. Check flood zone designations — a home in FEMA Zone AE carries mandatory flood insurance that can add $2,000–$6,000 or more per year to your cost of ownership, a number that should absolutely affect your renegotiation position.

> **Your agent works for the commission. We work for you. Know what you are buying before you sign anything you cannot undo.**

## Use Property Data to Strengthen Your Negotiating Position

The buyers who negotiate the best outcomes after inspections are the ones who show up with data, not just feelings. Before you even submit your repair request, pull the property's permit history. If the seller added a bathroom addition without pulling a permit, that is an unpermitted structure — a liability the inspection report may flag but cannot fully price. If the roof was replaced five years ago but no permit was ever pulled, you have no guarantee the work was done to code. Check whether the property has prior insurance claims through CLUE report data, which often reveals water damage or other recurring issues the seller is not volunteering. Cross-reference the inspection findings with comparable sales in the neighborhood to understand whether the seller has priced the property as if it were in perfect condition — and whether your renegotiation target is reasonable relative to the market.
