# How to Find Unfinaled Permits on a Los Angeles Property Before Buying

Unfinaled permits are building permits that were pulled and work was started — but an inspector never signed off on the finished job. In Los Angeles, you can check permit status for free using the Los Angeles Department of Building and Safety (LADBS) online portal at ladbs.org. Search by address, pull every open permit, and look for any record showing a status other than 'Finaled' or 'Closed.' If you find one, you are potentially inheriting tens of thousands of dollars in liability the seller never disclosed.

The short answer is: go to ladbs.org, click 'Check Permit & Inspection Status,' type in the property address, and download every permit record. Then cross-reference what you see on paper with what you see in person. A room addition with no permit record, or a permit record with no final inspection date, is a red flag that should go straight to your attorney — not your agent, who has a commission riding on this deal closing.

## Why Unfinaled Permits Are a Bigger Deal in Los Angeles Than Almost Anywhere Else

Los Angeles is one of the most permit-heavy cities in the country. The city issued over 44,000 building permits in fiscal year 2022 alone. That volume, combined with a chronic backlog of inspections, means open and unfinaled permits accumulate fast. When work is done without a final inspection — whether it's a garage conversion, a kitchen remodel, an ADU, or a pool — that work is legally considered incomplete in the eyes of the city. The structure may be perfectly safe, or it may have been built in a way that violates current fire, structural, or electrical codes. You have no way of knowing without an inspector's sign-off.

The real danger: when you close, those open permits transfer with the property. You become responsible for resolving them. In some cases that means paying for re-inspection. In others it means tearing out walls so an inspector can see the work that was done. In the worst cases, unpermitted additions must be demolished entirely. Los Angeles code enforcement can also issue a Notice of Non-Compliance, which gets recorded against the title and can block you from refinancing or selling later.

> **In Los Angeles, open permits don't stay with the seller. They transfer to you at closing — along with every dollar of liability attached to them.**

## Step-by-Step: How to Search LADBS for Open Permits

1. Go to ladbs.org and navigate to the 'Check Permit & Inspection Status' tool under the Building & Safety section.
2. Enter the full property address — use the format the city uses, which may differ from how it appears on Zillow or the MLS listing.
3. Review every permit record. Pay close attention to the 'Status' column. You want to see 'Finaled' or 'Closed.' Any status reading 'Issued,' 'Approved,' 'In Review,' or 'Expired' is an open or unresolved permit.
4. Note the permit type and date issued. A permit issued 10 years ago with no final inspection is a serious red flag.
5. Download or screenshot every result. Bring this to your home inspector and ask them to cross-reference the work described in each permit with what exists on the property.
6. If you find discrepancies — work that appears done but has no permit, or a permit for work that doesn't seem to exist — escalate to a real estate attorney immediately.
7. For complex histories, hire a permit expediter. They know how to read LADBS records and can often tell you exactly how expensive a resolution will be before you close.

## What LADBS Records Won't Tell You (And What to Do About It)

The LADBS portal shows permitted work — meaning work someone bothered to pull a permit for. It cannot show you work that was done without any permit at all. Unpermitted work has no record because no one filed for it. This is actually more common in Los Angeles than open permits. Estimates from California real estate attorneys suggest that somewhere between 25% and 40% of home additions and garage conversions in the greater LA area were done without permits at some point over the last 40 years.

To catch unpermitted work that never generated a record, you need a combination of approaches: a thorough home inspector who flags work that looks inconsistent with the original structure, a review of aerial imagery over time to spot additions, and a comparison of the permitted square footage on record with the actual measured square footage of the home. If a property is listed at 2,200 square feet but LADBS records only show permits covering a 1,400 square foot original structure, someone built 800 square feet without telling the city.

## The Specific Permit Types That Cause the Most Problems for LA Buyers

- Garage conversions and ADUs: Los Angeles has aggressively promoted ADU construction, but thousands of garage-to-dwelling conversions were done informally before ADU laws were liberalized. Many were never finaled and don't meet current habitability standards.
- Pool and spa permits: Pool permits frequently go unfinaled because owners never called for the final inspection after landscaping was completed. Lenders — especially FHA and VA — often flag these during underwriting.
- Electrical panel upgrades: A panel upgrade without a final inspection means no one verified that the new panel was installed safely. This is a fire risk and an insurance issue.
- Room additions and patio enclosures: Enclosed patios are one of the most common sources of unfinaled permits in LA. They get built, lived in for years, and no one ever noticed the permit was still open.
- Roof replacements: Roofing permits in LA require a final inspection to confirm the work meets current code, particularly for fire-resistant materials in high-risk zones.
- Solar installations: A growing category. Solar permits pulled after 2018 must meet specific interconnection and structural requirements. An unfinaled solar permit can create complications with your utility and your insurance carrier.

## How to Use Permit History as a Negotiation Tool, Not Just a Risk Screen

Here is what most buyers miss: unfinaled permits are not just a reason to walk away. They are leverage. If you find two open permits on a property — say, an unfinaled kitchen remodel from 2017 and an unpermitted bathroom addition — you can quantify the cost to resolve both and negotiate that amount off the purchase price, or require the seller to resolve them before close of escrow. A permit expediter in Los Angeles typically charges between $1,500 and $5,000 to resolve a single unfinaled permit, depending on complexity. A required demolition or rebuild of non-compliant work can run $15,000 to $50,000 or more.

Get a written estimate from a licensed contractor or permit expediter during your inspection period. Bring the estimate to the negotiating table. Your agent may push back — closing this deal is in their financial interest, not necessarily yours. Stand your ground. If the seller won't negotiate and won't resolve open permits, the liability is real and you have every right to cancel during your contingency period.
