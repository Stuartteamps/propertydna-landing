# Scottsdale AZ Building Permit Closure Status Check 2026: What Buyers Must Know Before They Close

If you are buying a home in Scottsdale, Arizona in 2026, checking the building permit closure status on that property is not optional — it is one of the most important due diligence steps you can take. An open, expired, or never-finaled permit means the work was never officially approved by a city inspector, which can leave you legally and financially responsible for bringing the structure up to code the moment you sign the deed. The short answer: you can look up permit history for any Scottsdale address through the City of Scottsdale's online Development Services portal, but knowing what you are looking at — and what is missing — is where most buyers get burned.

Scottsdale processed more than 28,000 building permit applications in a recent 12-month period, and a meaningful share of those permits are never formally closed out. Sellers are not always required to disclose open permits, and real estate agents — who are paid on commission at closing — have little incentive to slow a deal down by flagging one. ## Why Open Permits Are a Big Deal in Scottsdale

When a contractor pulls a building permit in Scottsdale and does the work but never schedules the final inspection, the permit stays open indefinitely. The city does not automatically chase down every unfinished permit. Years later — sometimes decades — that open permit is still sitting in the record, tied to the property, not the person who ordered the work. When you buy that house, you inherit it. The City of Scottsdale can require you to obtain a final inspection or, worse, require demolition and reconstruction if the work does not meet current code. Arizona homeowners have faced remediation bills ranging from a few hundred dollars for a water heater permit to well over $30,000 for unpermitted room additions that required structural correction.

> **Open permits do not belong to the seller. The moment you close, they belong to you — and so does the bill to fix whatever a city inspector finds wrong.**

## How to Check Permit Status Directly Through Scottsdale's City Portal

The City of Scottsdale's Development Services department maintains a public permit search tool. Here is how to use it in 2026. Navigate to the city's online permit portal — search 'Scottsdale AZ permit search' to find the current URL, as the city occasionally migrates systems. Once there, you can search by address, permit number, contractor name, or parcel number. Enter the full property address and look at every permit record that comes up, not just the most recent one. You are looking for the status field on each record.

- Finaled or Closed: The permit received a passing final inspection. This is what you want to see.
- Expired: The permit was issued but work was never completed or inspected within the allowed timeframe — typically 180 days in Scottsdale without an approved extension.
- Issued / Active: A permit was pulled and work may or may not be complete, but no final inspection has been recorded.
- Void: The permit was cancelled, which can mean the project was abandoned. Understand why.
- No Permit Found: Does not mean no work was done — it can mean work was done without a permit at all, which is often worse.

## What to Do When You Find an Open or Expired Permit

Finding an open or expired permit during your Scottsdale home purchase is not automatically a deal-killer, but it is a negotiating lever and a liability you must understand before proceeding. Your first move is to contact Scottsdale's Development Services at 480-312-2500 — the actual permit desk, not a general city line — and ask specifically what is required to close out that permit today. Get the answer in writing if you can. Inspectors will tell you exactly what final steps, inspections, or corrections are needed. From there, you have three options: require the seller to close the permit before closing escrow, negotiate a price reduction equal to the estimated cost of closure, or walk away if the scope is too large or too uncertain.

Do not accept a verbal promise from the seller's agent that the permit 'will be closed before closing.' Make it a written contingency in your purchase contract with a hard deadline. In Arizona, the standard AAR Residential Resale Real Estate Purchase Contract allows for inspection contingencies, but open permit resolution must be explicitly written in — it is not assumed.

## Unpermitted Work Is Worse Than an Open Permit
