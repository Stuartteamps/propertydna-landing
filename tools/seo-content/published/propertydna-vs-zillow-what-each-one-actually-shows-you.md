# PropertyDNA vs Zillow — What Each One Actually Shows You

Zillow is a listing platform. PropertyDNA is a due-diligence platform. Both let you look up any address in seconds, but they were built for completely different purposes — and understanding that difference could save you from a six-figure mistake. Zillow wants you to find a home and contact an agent. PropertyDNA wants you to know what you're actually buying before you wire the money.

The short answer: Zillow shows you the property the seller wants you to see. PropertyDNA shows you the property the seller hopes you won't look too hard at — permit gaps, flood designations, price history that predates the current listing, and risk layers that real estate portals have little incentive to surface. If you're doing serious homework on a home, you need both — but for very different reasons.

## What Zillow Was Built to Do (And Who It's Really For)

Zillow's business model is selling leads to real estate agents. In 2023, Zillow reported over $1.9 billion in revenue — the vast majority of it from agent and lender advertising. That's not a criticism; it's just the economic reality that shapes every design decision on the platform. The photos are large and gorgeous. The map is smooth. The mortgage calculator is front-and-center. Everything nudges you toward clicking 'Contact Agent.' Zillow is phenomenally good at what it was designed to do: connect buyers who are emotionally ready to transact with professionals who want that transaction to happen.

- Current MLS listing data — photos, square footage, listed price, days on market
- Zestimate (an algorithmic price estimate with a national median error rate of around 2.4% on listed homes, but significantly higher off-market)
- Nearby sold comps and neighborhood price trends
- School ratings sourced from GreatSchools
- Basic walk score and transit data
- Agent contact forms and lender ads

> **Zillow is optimized for discovery. PropertyDNA is optimized for protection. Know which mode you're in.**

## What PropertyDNA Was Built to Do (And Who It's Really For)

PropertyDNA was built on a single premise: homebuyers have always been the least-informed party in the most expensive transaction of their lives. Agents represent themselves and their commission. Sellers disclose only what the law forces them to. Listing platforms profit when deals close, not when buyers make smart decisions. PropertyDNA flips that. The platform aggregates permit records, FEMA flood zone designations, tax assessment history, price event timelines, environmental risk layers, and ownership chain data — then puts it in front of the buyer in plain language, not buried in county databases that require a law degree to navigate.

- Building permit history — what was permitted, what wasn't, and when
- FEMA flood zone classification (AE, X, VE and others) and flood event proximity
- Full price history including reductions, re-listings, and pre-MLS transactions
- Tax assessment vs. listing price divergence — a key overprice signal
- Ownership chain and holding period data
- Environmental and natural hazard risk scoring
- Zoning data and any recorded liens or encumbrances

## The Permit Gap Problem Zillow Won't Tell You About

Here's a concrete example of the gap between these two tools. A home lists on Zillow with a finished basement adding 600 square feet to the livable area. The photos look great. The price per square foot looks competitive. What Zillow cannot tell you — and has no incentive to investigate — is whether that basement finish was ever permitted by the local building department. Unpermitted work is one of the most common landmines in residential real estate. Depending on the jurisdiction, you could inherit the liability: forced demolition of the work, failed refinance appraisals, and insurance claim denials. PropertyDNA surfaces permit records directly, flagging when major renovations don't have corresponding permit activity. That one data point alone can change your offer price or your decision to walk.

## Flood Risk: What a Flood Zone Label Actually Means for Your Wallet

Zillow does display a basic flood factor score on many listings, sourced from First Street Foundation. It's better than nothing. But PropertyDNA goes further, pulling the actual FEMA flood zone designation — the one your lender and insurance provider will use. There's a massive practical difference between an AE designation (high-risk, mandatory federal flood insurance required if you have a federally-backed mortgage) and an X designation (minimal risk, no mandatory insurance). In high-risk zones, flood insurance alone can cost $2,000 to $5,000 per year or more depending on the property's elevation certificate and coverage level. That's a cost that can swing your monthly payment by hundreds of dollars and blow up your debt-to-income ratio. Zillow's listing page won't calculate that for you. PropertyDNA flags the zone and puts the cost implication in context.

> **A flood zone designation isn't a footnote. In some markets, it's the difference between a property that pencils and one that bleeds you dry.**

## Price History — The Story Zillow Tells vs. The One It Skips

Zillow does show price history — but only from the point the home entered the MLS. If a home was listed, pulled, relisted under a new MLS number, or sold off-market before the current listing, that history often disappears or appears incomplete. PropertyDNA stitches together the fuller picture: prior arms-length sales, tax-assessed value trends over five or more years, and price reduction events even when a listing was refreshed to reset the 'days on market' counter. That last tactic — pulling a stale listing and relaunching it as 'new' — is extremely common in slower markets. A buyer looking only at Zillow might see a home with 12 days on market. PropertyDNA might show you that same home has been unsuccessfully marketed for 190 days across three listing cycles. That changes your negotiating leverage entirely.

## When to Use Zillow vs. When to Use PropertyDNA

These tools aren't competitors in the way that, say, two mapping apps compete. They serve different stages of the buying process. Use Zillow early — it's the best consumer interface for browsing inventory, setting saved searches, and getting a feel for what's available in your price range and target neighborhoods. Use PropertyDNA before you make an offer on any specific property. Think of Zillow as the showroom and PropertyDNA as the inspection report you wish existed before you ever stepped inside. Smart buyers use both. Buyers who skip PropertyDNA are relying on a platform that profits from the transaction closing — not from the transaction being the right one for them.

- Use Zillow for: browsing active inventory, tracking neighborhood price trends, setting alerts for new listings
- Use PropertyDNA for: pre-offer due diligence, permit history, flood risk, full price event history, risk scoring before committing
- Use both together: identify candidates on Zillow, vet each one with a PropertyDNA report before you get emotionally attached
