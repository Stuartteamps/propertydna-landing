# Why Your Buyer's Agent Works for the Seller (Not You)

Your buyer's agent is not your advocate. That's not an opinion — it's baked into how they get paid. In the traditional real estate model, your buyer's agent earns a commission (typically 2.5–3% of the purchase price) that is funded by the seller and only paid when a deal closes. No deal, no paycheck. That single fact creates a conflict of interest so fundamental it should be printed on every buyer's agency agreement in bold type. Your agent needs you to buy a house. Whether it's the right house, at the right price, with the right disclosures — those are secondary concerns.

This doesn't mean every buyer's agent is acting in bad faith. Many are genuinely trying to help. But good intentions don't override financial incentives. The structure of the transaction means your agent is economically aligned with the seller's agent, the seller's lender, and the title company — everyone except you. The 2024 NAR settlement changed some of the rules around how commissions are advertised, but it did not change the fundamental truth: the person sitting next to you at the closing table has been working, consciously or not, toward a commission check the entire time.

## The Commission Structure Explains Everything

Here's the math most buyers never see. On a $450,000 home, a 2.5% buyer's agent commission is $11,250. That money comes out of the seller's proceeds — which means it was baked into the listing price from day one. The seller's agent and the buyer's agent split a total commission pot, often 5–6% of the sale price. Both agents get paid only at closing. Both agents lose money if you walk away from a deal. That means both agents — even though one technically represents you — share a financial interest in the transaction completing, regardless of whether it's good for you.

> **The agent who 'represents' you gets paid by the seller. That's not a technicality. That's the whole game.**

## What Fiduciary Duty Actually Means (and Doesn't)

Buyer's agents are legally required to act in your fiduciary interest in most U.S. states. That sounds protective. In practice, fiduciary duty in real estate means your agent cannot lie to you outright, cannot share your private negotiating position with the seller, and must disclose known material defects. What it does not mean: your agent must warn you that the neighborhood has had 14 code violations filed in the last 18 months. It doesn't require them to pull permit history, flag insurance claim records, or tell you the seller relisted after a failed inspection with a different buyer. That information exists — your agent just isn't required to find it, and finding it doesn't help them close faster.

## Dual Agency: When the Conflict Becomes Explicit

Dual agency is when one agent — or two agents at the same brokerage — represents both the buyer and the seller in the same transaction. In states where it's legal, this arrangement is disclosed but rarely explained. Dual agency is legal in 41 states. In a dual-agency situation, your agent is contractually prohibited from advocating for your interests over the seller's. They become a neutral facilitator. But they earn both sides of the commission — sometimes the full 5–6% on a single transaction. As a buyer, you're expected to negotiate the most important purchase of your life against a seller, with the same person in the middle whose paycheck doubles if you overpay.

- Dual agency is legal in 41 states — your agent may not mention it until they're legally required to disclose it
- In dual agency, your agent cannot advise you on pricing strategy, negotiation tactics, or whether the price is fair
- The agent's commission can double in dual-agency deals — making it the most financially rewarding outcome for them
- Some buyers are steered toward listings from the same brokerage without knowing both sides are represented internally
- Even in non-dual-agency deals, agents at competing brokerages are incentivized to close — not to protect you

## The Information Gap Your Agent Isn't Closing

Real estate agents have access to the MLS. They can show you sold comps, active listings, and days on market. That's useful, but it's surface-level data. What most agents won't — or can't — bring to you: building permit history and open permits that could signal unpermitted work or unfinished construction, flood zone designations (a property in FEMA Zone AE carries mandatory flood insurance that can cost $2,000–$4,000 per year, a fact that changes your effective carrying cost entirely), prior insurance claims filed on the property, ownership transfer patterns that suggest distressed sales or title issues, and local code violation filings that hint at structural or habitability problems. Your agent is not a data analyst. They're a licensed salesperson. And salesperson isn't an insult — it's just a job description that doesn't include forensic due diligence on your behalf.

## How the 2024 NAR Settlement Changed (and Didn't Change) Things

After a landmark antitrust verdict and the resulting $418 million NAR settlement, the rules around buyer's agent compensation were restructured starting in August 2024. Sellers are no longer required to offer a buyer's agent commission through the MLS. Buyers must now sign a written agreement with their agent before touring homes, and that agreement must specify the agent's compensation. This is genuinely a step toward transparency. But it doesn't change the underlying incentive. If you agree to pay your agent 2.5% to represent you, they still earn that money only at closing. The pressure to get you across the finish line hasn't disappeared — it's just been disclosed a little more formally. The structural conflict remains.

> **The NAR settlement changed who writes the check. It didn't change who the check rewards for closing the deal.**

## What Actual Buyer Protection Looks Like

Protecting yourself as a buyer starts with independent property intelligence — data gathered on your behalf before you fall in love with a house and lose negotiating leverage. Before you make an offer on any property, you should know: the full permit history and any open or expired permits, whether the property sits in a flood zone or high-risk wildfire area, any prior insurance claims on the structure, the ownership and transaction history (how many times has it sold and why), and how the asking price compares to objective data — not just what your agent says comps support. None of this information costs you more than your agent's commission to obtain. Most of it is available in public records your agent has no legal obligation to surface for you. The gap between what exists and what you're shown is exactly where buyers get hurt.

- Always request a full permit history from the county building department before making an offer
- Check FEMA flood maps independently — your agent is not required to explain the cost of flood insurance
- Ask directly: 'Is this a dual-agency or in-house listing?' before your agent shows you any property
- Hire an independent inspector — not one your agent recommends
- Use independent data platforms to verify pricing, risk, and history before you fall in love with a property
- Read your buyer's agency agreement before signing — understand exactly how and when your agent gets paid
