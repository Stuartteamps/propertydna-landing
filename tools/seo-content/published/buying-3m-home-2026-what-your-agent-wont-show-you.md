# Buying a $3M Home in 2026 — What Your Agent Won't Show You

When you're buying a $3 million home, you'd assume the process is more sophisticated than a starter purchase — better data, more transparency, sharper advisors. The uncomfortable reality is the opposite. At the $3M price point, information asymmetry doesn't shrink. It compounds. The seller has lived in the property, hired the contractors, pulled (or quietly not pulled) the permits, and chosen which disclosures to make. Your agent, no matter how polished, is paid a commission that closes only when you buy — typically 2.5% to 3%, or $75,000 to $90,000 on a $3M transaction. That's not an accusation. It's just math worth understanding before you wire seven figures.

The good news: institutional-grade property intelligence that was once reserved for hedge funds and REITs is now available to individual buyers. Before you make your highest offer, here is exactly what your agent has no structural incentive to surface — and how to find it yourself.

## The Permit History Is the X-Ray of the Property

A $3M home has almost certainly been renovated, expanded, or updated — often more than once. Every one of those projects should have a corresponding building permit, final inspection sign-off, and certificate of occupancy where applicable. In practice, luxury sellers routinely complete work without pulling permits, or pull permits that were never finaled. That remodeled kitchen, the added square footage, the pool-to-spa conversion — if no permit exists, that work is legally unpermitted and may need to be torn out at your expense. Municipalities in California, Florida, and Texas flag unpermitted structures during resale inspections and can issue stop-work orders on future projects. One open permit from 2017 can delay your closing by 60 days or kill your financing entirely. Pull the permit history on any address before you submit an offer, not after.

## Flood Zone Designations Have Changed — and Your Premium Reflects It

FEMA remapped significant portions of coastal and inland flood zones between 2020 and 2025. A property that carried a standard homeowner's policy three years ago may now sit in a Special Flood Hazard Area (SFHA), specifically FEMA Zone AE or VE — the highest-risk designations. Flood insurance in a Zone VE coastal property can run $18,000 to $40,000 per year on a $3M home, and that number is not included in any listing's advertised carrying costs. Worse, the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) has caps on coverage — $250,000 for the structure — meaning a luxury buyer must layer private market flood coverage on top, adding complexity and cost that your agent almost certainly hasn't modeled into your monthly payment estimate.

> **Your agent's job ends at the commission. Your financial exposure begins the day you close. Know your flood zone before you know your offer price.**

## The Seller's Disclosure Is a Legal Floor, Not a Full Picture

Every state mandates some version of a seller's disclosure — a form the seller fills out listing known defects. At the luxury level, sellers are often represented by sophisticated listing agents who understand exactly how to answer disclosure questions truthfully while revealing as little as possible. 'No known defects' is a legally defensible statement even if the seller suspects foundation movement, has had water intrusion, or received a prior inspection with flagged items they chose not to repair. Your legal recourse after closing — when you discover the issue — is expensive, slow, and uncertain. The better play is to use independent data sources to verify what the disclosure doesn't say. Claims history on a property address, prior listing photos showing water stains, permit records showing repeated roof permits — all of this exists in public records and third-party databases. You just have to know where to look.

## Days on Market Is Being Gamed — Especially at This Price Point

Luxury listings are routinely withdrawn and relisted to reset the days-on-market (DOM) counter — a tactic so common it has an industry nickname: 'washing the listing.' A home that has been sitting unsold for 200 days can reappear as a fresh 2-day listing after a brief withdrawal. At the $3M price point, the psychology is deliberate: buyers assume a new listing is competitively priced, which creates false urgency. The actual cumulative market time on a property tells you something critical — the market has already passed on this asset at or near this price, multiple times. Knowing total listing history, including prior list prices, helps you calibrate your offer and your leverage. In most markets, a luxury home that has been on-and-off the market for 9+ months represents negotiating power of 5% to 12% below current ask.

## HOA and Special Assessment Risk Is Underestimated at Every Price

Luxury properties frequently sit within HOAs, private communities, or high-rise buildings with shared infrastructure. What buyers rarely see before closing: the HOA reserve fund balance, the deferred maintenance schedule, and any pending or recently levied special assessments. A $3M condo in a building with an underfunded reserve and aging elevators, a failing facade, or a newly mandated structural inspection (common in Florida post-Surfside) could carry a pending special assessment of $80,000 to $300,000 per unit — due within 90 days of being levied. Florida's SB 4-D legislation now requires milestone inspections and reserve studies for condo buildings three stories or taller. Ask for the most recent reserve study, the last two years of meeting minutes, and written confirmation of any pending assessments. If your agent hasn't asked for these documents proactively, that's information.

- Request the full HOA reserve study, not just the current monthly dues
- Ask for 24 months of HOA board meeting minutes — votes on assessments, litigation, and deferred repairs are documented there
- Confirm in writing whether any special assessments have been levied or are under discussion
- Verify the HOA's current litigation status — buyers inherit the building's legal exposure
- For Florida condos: confirm milestone inspection compliance under SB 4-D

## Environmental and Hazard Risk Doesn't Respect Price Tags

Wildfire, subsidence, sea level rise, radon, and legacy environmental contamination don't care that you paid $3 million. In California, parcels in High or Very High Fire Hazard Severity Zones (FHSZ) are increasingly uninsurable through standard carriers — and as of 2025, the California FAIR Plan itself has coverage limits that fall short of the replacement cost of a luxury home. In some hillside communities, buyers discovered post-purchase that their insurer had quietly non-renewed the prior owner's policy, triggering a coverage gap. Separately, subsidence risk — gradual sinking of the land itself — affects coastal and hillside properties in ways that don't show up in standard inspections but do appear in geological survey data. A property intelligence report layers these environmental datasets against a specific parcel so you understand the risk profile before you're holding the keys.

## What to Do Before You Make an Offer on Any $3M Property

1. Pull a full property DNA report on the address — permit history, ownership chain, flood zone, prior listing history, and tax record anomalies in one place
2. Verify the total cumulative days on market across all listing periods, not just the current MLS entry

6. Check for open or expired permits and confirm all prior work was finaled by the local building department
7. Verify current insurability with at least two independent carriers before your inspection contingency expires
