# Dual Agency Real Estate: What Every Buyer Needs to Know in 2026

Dual agency happens when a single real estate agent — or two agents from the same brokerage — represents both the buyer and the seller in the same transaction. On paper, it sounds efficient. In practice, it is one of the most significant conflicts of interest a homebuyer can face. The short answer: dual agency almost always benefits the agent and the seller, not you.

Here is why it matters in 2026 more than ever. Following the National Association of Realtors settlement that restructured buyer-agent compensation rules in mid-2024, more buyers are signing formal representation agreements upfront — and more agents are quietly steering those buyers toward their own listings to earn both sides of the commission. That can be as much as 5 to 6 percent of the purchase price collected by a single agent or brokerage on a single deal. On a $450,000 home, that is $22,500 to $27,000 flowing to one party who is supposed to be looking out for two completely opposing interests at once.

## What Dual Agency Actually Means — and What It Costs You

A buyer's agent owes you fiduciary duty: loyalty, confidentiality, full disclosure, and the obligation to negotiate aggressively on your behalf. The moment that same agent also represents the seller, every one of those duties is compromised. The agent cannot tell you the seller is desperate and would accept $30,000 below asking, because that information belongs to the seller. The agent cannot push hard on repairs after inspection, because that creates friction for the seller. The agent cannot advise you to walk away, because losing the deal means losing both commissions. You are legally entitled to 'neutral' representation — which is another way of saying you are entitled to almost nothing.

> **Dual agency doesn't just reduce your leverage — it eliminates it. You can't negotiate hard when the person doing the negotiating gets paid more if you pay more.**

## Is Dual Agency Legal in Your State in 2026?

Dual agency is legal in most U.S. states, provided the agent discloses it in writing and both parties give informed consent. However, eight states have taken stronger positions. Alaska, Colorado, Florida, Kansas, Maryland, Oklahoma, Texas, and Vermont either ban true dual agency outright or require a sharply limited form called designated agency, where two separate agents within the same brokerage each represent one party. The catch with designated agency: the supervising broker still benefits from both sides of the deal, and agents in the same office routinely share information, office culture, and — critically — income incentives tied to volume. Disclosure requirements vary dramatically by state, so the form you sign may bury the conflict in legal language on page four.

- California: Dual agency is legal with written disclosure; agents must disclose both agency relationships before the buyer makes an offer.
- New York: Dual agency allowed; designated agency is common at large brokerages — verify your agent's exact role in writing.
- Texas: True dual agency is prohibited; intermediary relationships are permitted with limited representation duties for both parties.
- Florida: Dual agency allowed with written consent; transaction brokerage is the default relationship, which already limits fiduciary duty.
- Colorado: Dual agency banned since 2003 — one of the strongest buyer-protection states in the country.

## The Warning Signs You Are Being Steered Into a Dual Agency Situation

Steering is subtle. Your agent may not say 'hey, I also list this property.' Instead, watch for these patterns: the agent consistently shows you listings from their own brokerage first; they express unusual enthusiasm for one specific property while being lukewarm on others; they tell you a competing offer is coming in — a claim you have no way to verify — to push you toward a faster decision; or they discourage you from making a lower offer on a property where they hold the listing. None of these behaviors are illegal on their own. Combined with a dual-agency disclosure form that appeared at the last minute, they form a pattern worth questioning directly.

## How to Protect Yourself Before You Sign Anything

- Ask your agent upfront: 'Do you or anyone at your brokerage hold any active listings we might view?' Get this answered before you sign a buyer representation agreement.
- Read the representation agreement for language about dual agency or designated agency consent — if it is pre-checked or buried, push back.
- Search the listing independently. Property records, permit history, and listing history are public. Know what you are walking into before the showing.
- If a dual agency situation arises mid-search, you have the right to hire a different agent for that specific property — even if you have a representation agreement. Check the exit clauses.
- Use independent data tools to understand the property's actual market value, flood risk, permit status, and price history before entering negotiations. That is information a dual agent has no incentive to surface for you.
- Request the seller's disclosure documents before making an offer, and read them yourself — do not rely on your agent's summary.
- If you are in a state where dual agency is legal and you are pressured to consent, consult a real estate attorney. A one-hour consultation typically costs $150 to $300 and can save you tens of thousands.

## What the 2024 NAR Settlement Changed — and What It Didn't

The August 2024 NAR settlement required buyers to sign written representation agreements before touring homes, and prohibited listing agents from advertising buyer-agent compensation on MLS systems. The goal was to make buyer-agent fees more transparent and negotiable. The unintended consequence: some buyers, confused or cost-conscious, have chosen to go unrepresented — or have worked directly with the listing agent to avoid paying a separate buyer-agent fee. In both cases, the buyer ends up with either no professional representation or a dual-agency relationship, both of which expose them to the same information asymmetry the old system created. The settlement restructured who pays what; it did not eliminate the structural conflict inside dual agency.

> **The NAR settlement changed how agents get paid. It did not change the fact that an agent earning both sides of a commission has a financial interest in your transaction that competes directly with yours.**
