# What Is the 8 Percent Comp Spread Rule for Home Buyers?

The 8 percent comp spread rule is a buyer-side valuation guideline that flags a property as potentially overpriced when its list price sits more than 8 percent above the median of its three most relevant comparable sales — adjusted for square footage, condition, and proximity. In plain terms: if the comps say a home is worth $400,000 and it's listed at $435,000 or higher, you've crossed the spread threshold and need to understand exactly why before you write a check.

This rule didn't come from the NAR or any government agency. It emerged from institutional investors and appraisers who needed a fast, repeatable screen to separate legitimate pricing from aspirational pricing. Most buyers never hear about it because their agent is paid on commission — a higher sale price means a bigger paycheck for them. ## Why 8 Percent Specifically?

The 8 percent threshold isn't arbitrary. Appraisers working for lenders typically allow a plus-or-minus 5 to 10 percent variance when reconciling comparable sales into a final value opinion, and institutional buyers use a similar band when underwriting single-family acquisitions. Eight percent sits right in the middle of that range — tight enough to catch genuine overpricing, wide enough not to trigger false alarms on homes with legitimate premium features like a renovated kitchen, a larger lot, or a better school zone. When a listing exceeds that band without a documented justification for each point of variance, the spread becomes your signal to negotiate hard or walk away.

## How to Actually Run a Comp Spread Analysis

Running the calculation yourself takes five steps, and you don't need an appraisal license to do it. The goal is to build a small, tight comp set and measure the gap between those comps and what the seller is asking.

1. Pull 3 to 5 closed sales within 0.5 miles of the target property that closed within the last 90 days.
2. Filter to homes within 15 percent of the target's square footage — a 1,800 sq ft home compared to a 2,600 sq ft home isn't a real comp.
3. Adjust for obvious condition differences: a fully renovated home typically commands $20 to $40 per square foot more than an unrenovated comparable in most mid-tier markets.
4. Calculate the median price-per-square-foot across your comp set, then multiply by the target home's square footage to get your baseline value.
5. Divide the list price by your baseline value, subtract 1, and convert to a percentage. Anything above 8 percent is your trigger to dig deeper or negotiate.

> **When the spread exceeds 8 percent and the seller can't justify it with documented upgrades or lot premium, you're not negotiating — you're rescuing the seller from a mispriced listing.**

## What a Spread Above 8 Percent Actually Means in Practice

A positive spread above 8 percent doesn't automatically mean you should skip the property. It means you need a documented reason for the gap. Sellers often overprice for three reasons: they bought at peak and need to recoup, their agent set an aspirational list price to win the listing, or they've made improvements that feel more valuable to them than to the market. In competitive markets like parts of South Florida and coastal California, sellers tested buyer appetite in 2022 with spreads as high as 15 to 20 percent above comps — and sometimes got it. By late 2024, those same markets saw average days-on-market stretch past 60 days for homes priced more than 8 percent above the adjusted comp median, according to aggregated MLS trend data. The market is a much harsher judge than a motivated seller.

## The Danger Zones: When Comp Spread Becomes a Financing Problem

Here's the piece your agent definitely won't bring up until it's too late: if a home appraises below contract price, your lender will only finance based on the appraised value, not what you agreed to pay. So if you're under contract at $435,000 on a home that appraises at $400,000, you either need to cover the $35,000 gap in cash, renegotiate the price down, or walk — assuming your contract included an appraisal contingency. Buyers who waived that contingency in competitive markets and then discovered a negative spread at appraisal have lost their earnest money deposits, sometimes $5,000 to $15,000 or more, because they didn't run a basic comp spread check before writing the offer. Knowing the spread before you offer gives you the power to build the right contingencies in from the start.

## Common Mistakes Buyers Make When Pulling Their Own Comps

- Using Zillow's Zestimate as a comp — it's an automated model, not a comparable sale, and it can lag real market movement by 30 to 60 days or more.
- Comparing across school district lines — a home one block outside a desirable district can trade at 8 to 12 percent less than an identical home inside it.
- Ignoring permit history — a renovated kitchen with no permits pulls value down, not up, and won't show in a surface-level comp comparison.
- Including active listings in your comp set — only closed sales represent what the market actually paid, not what sellers hoped to get.
- Stretching the time window past 90 days in fast-moving markets — in a market where values shifted 5 percent in a quarter, a 6-month-old comp is essentially fiction.
- Forgetting to adjust for lot size in suburban and rural markets where land value can swing price by $10,000 to $50,000 or more.
