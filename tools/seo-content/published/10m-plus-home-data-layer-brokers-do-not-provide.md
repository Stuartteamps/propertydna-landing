## Why Your Luxury Agent's Disclosure Packet Is Not Enough

A seller's disclosure is a legal document prepared by the seller. Read that sentence again. At the $10M+ level, sellers retain attorneys to craft disclosures that are technically accurate but strategically minimal. Studies of residential real estate litigation consistently show that unpermitted construction and undisclosed material defects are among the top two causes of post-closing lawsuits — even on high-end properties. Your agent reviews that disclosure, but they are not a permit researcher, a flood engineer, or a title historian. They are a salesperson. That is not an insult — it is a structural fact about how the industry is built and compensated.

## Permit History: The Record That Exposes What Was Built Without Permission

Luxury properties are renovated constantly. Wine cellars get added. Guest houses get expanded. Rooftop terraces get enclosed. At the ultra-high-end, renovation scopes routinely exceed $1 million — and a surprising portion of that work is performed without permits, or with permits that were opened and never closed. An open permit can cloud title, complicate your insurance, and create personal liability if an unpermitted structure later causes injury or fire damage. In Los Angeles County alone, the Department of Building and Safety carries tens of thousands of open permit records at any given time. When you pull a property's permit history before making an offer, you are not being difficult — you are being rational.

- Closed permits confirm work was inspected and approved by the municipality
- Open permits signal work was started but never signed off — a red flag for lenders and insurers
- Missing permits on additions or ADUs can mean square footage is not legally recognized
- Permit records also reveal contractor history, which can hint at construction quality
- Some jurisdictions flag code violations directly inside the permit record

## Flood Zone Designation: FEMA Maps Are Not Just for Beach Cottages

One of the most common misconceptions among luxury buyers is that flood risk is a coastal or entry-level problem. It is not. FEMA's National Flood Insurance Program designates properties into flood zones ranging from minimal-risk Zone X to high-risk Zone AE and VE. A Zone AE designation on a $14 million estate in a river valley, a hillside with a seasonal creek, or a coastal bluff in Florida can add $40,000 to $80,000 per year in flood insurance premiums — a carrying cost that the listing sheet will never volunteer. Beyond insurance, properties in Special Flood Hazard Areas face mortgage restrictions, mandatory disclosure laws in many states, and increasing resale difficulty as climate-aware buyers do their own research. Pull the FEMA flood map overlay before you fall in love with the architectural rendering.

## Lien and Encumbrance History: What Sits on the Title Before You Do

Title insurance is standard in luxury transactions, but title insurance is retroactive protection — it covers you after a problem surfaces, not before. The smarter move is to understand the encumbrance history of a property before you make an offer so you can negotiate accordingly or walk away. At the $10M+ tier, the lien landscape can be genuinely complex. Mechanics' liens from contractors on recent renovations may still be within their filing window. HOA super-priority liens in states like Nevada and Florida can survive foreclosure and attach to new ownership. Deed restrictions from original land grants or historic preservation agreements can limit your ability to develop, demolish, or even repaint. IRS federal tax liens follow the property until formally discharged. A title commitment ordered during escrow will catch these — but by then you have already spent money on inspections and appraisals.

> **Title insurance pays you after the problem. Property intelligence lets you see the problem before you wire a dollar.**

## Price History and Days on Market: The Negotiation Data Your Broker Glosses Over

The MLS price history for a luxury listing is one of the most revealing datasets available — and one of the most consistently buried in listing presentations. A $13.5 million property that was originally listed at $17 million eighteen months ago, pulled, relisted at $15.5 million, and then repriced twice more tells you an entirely different story than its current listing sheet suggests. At the ultra-luxury tier, properties with more than 180 days cumulative market exposure are statistically more likely to transact at 10-15% below final list price. Knowing that going into a negotiation changes everything about your opening offer, your contingency posture, and your walkaway number. This data exists in public MLS archives and county assessor sales records — it just requires someone to actually pull it and present it to you.

- Original list price versus current price reveals true seller motivation
- Number of price reductions indicates market rejection and negotiation leverage
- Cumulative days on market — not just current listing days — is the honest exposure metric
- Prior sale dates and prices expose whether the seller is sitting on equity or underwater
- Off-market relisting gaps sometimes indicate failed deals worth investigating

## Environmental and Geologic Risk: The Layer No Staging Can Hide

California's Natural Hazard Disclosure law requires sellers to flag seismic, wildfire, and flood risk zones — but only for the specific zones defined by state statute. Dozens of meaningful risk categories fall outside mandatory disclosure. In states like Florida, Texas, and Colorado, environmental disclosures are even thinner. For a $10M+ property, the relevant questions include: Is the parcel within a mapped liquefaction or landslide zone? What is the wildfire risk score from a third-party model, not just a state map that was drawn in 2010? Are there any recorded environmental site assessments for prior commercial use of the land? Is the property within a PFAS groundwater contamination advisory area? These are not hypothetical concerns. They are the kinds of issues that surface in post-closing litigation, insurance claim denials, and resale difficulty — and they are addressable with publicly available data if someone assembles it for you before the contract is signed.
