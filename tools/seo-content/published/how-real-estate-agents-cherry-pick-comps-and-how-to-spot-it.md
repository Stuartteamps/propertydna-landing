# How Real Estate Agents Cherry-Pick Comps and How to Spot It

Real estate agents cherry-pick comps — comparable sales — by selectively choosing properties that support whatever price benefits them most. A listing agent wants the highest possible list price to justify their commission and win the listing. A buyer's agent wants to appear helpful but still has a financial incentive tied to closing a deal fast. The result: the comparable sales analysis you're shown is almost never a neutral document. It's a curated argument. Understanding the seven most common manipulation tactics — and knowing how to pull your own unfiltered data — is how you avoid overpaying by tens of thousands of dollars.

The uncomfortable truth is that comp selection is subjective enough to be gamed without technically lying. An agent can cherry-pick legally, ethically by industry standards, and still cost you $40,000 or more on a single transaction. Redfin research has found that homes sold with a seller's agent sell for roughly 5.6% more than FSBO homes — part of that premium is real market expertise, but part of it is manufactured price perception built through selective comp presentation. Here's exactly how it works and what you can do about it.

## The 7 Most Common Cherry-Picking Tactics

1. Radius creep: Pulling comps from a more expensive neighborhood half a mile away while ignoring cheaper sales directly next door.
2. Date shopping: Using a sale from 11 months ago when the market was hotter, ignoring the past 90 days of softening prices.
3. Square-footage bracket manipulation: Choosing only homes that are 200–300 sq ft smaller so the price-per-square-foot math inflates your target property's value.
4. Condition filtering: Including fully renovated homes as comps for a property that hasn't been touched since 1987.
5. Lot size cherry-picking: Highlighting large-lot comps for a home on a postage-stamp lot, or vice versa, depending on which direction the agent needs the number to go.
6. Pending vs. closed confusion: Using pending sales (which are hopes, not facts) instead of closed transactions to suggest prices are rising when they may be falling.
7. Omission of distressed sales: Quietly excluding short sales, estate sales, or foreclosures that dragged nearby prices down — all legitimate market data.

## Why Both Your Buyer's Agent and the Listing Agent Have a Motive to Skew High

Most buyers assume their agent is fighting for the lowest price. But buyer's agents are paid a percentage of the purchase price — typically 2.5% to 3% of whatever you pay. On a $600,000 home, your agent earns $15,000 to $18,000. If cherry-picked comps help justify a $640,000 offer instead of a $590,000 offer, your agent pockets an extra $1,500. That's not conspiracy — it's just misaligned incentives baked into the compensation structure. The listing agent's motive is even more obvious: they were hired by the seller specifically to get top dollar, and they won the listing partly by showing the seller flattering comps. Neither of these professionals is running an objective valuation exercise.

## How to Audit a CMA Yourself in Under 20 Minutes

You don't need MLS access or an appraisal license to stress-test a comp set. Start by taking every address in the CMA you were given and mapping them. If the comps cluster on one side of a major road, a school district boundary, or a flood zone line, that's a red flag — those invisible boundaries move prices dramatically. Next, go to your county property appraiser's website, which is public record in every U.S. state, and look up the actual sale dates. Agents often cite the list price or contract date rather than the recorded sale date, which can make a market look hotter than it is. Finally, count the omissions: ask your agent to show you every closed sale within a half-mile and 90-day window, then see which ones didn't make the CMA and ask why. The explanation for each exclusion tells you a lot about the analysis's integrity.

- Map all comps geographically — neighborhood boundaries matter more than zip codes.
- Verify closed dates on county records, not just what the CMA reports.
- Request the full unfiltered comp pull, not just the curated selection.
- Check price-per-square-foot for each comp against the subject property — outliers reveal the cherry-picking.
- Ask specifically: 'Were any sales excluded because they were distressed or estate sales?'
- Look at active listings too — if 6 similar homes are sitting unsold at $580,000, a comp set showing $620,000 closings from 8 months ago is misleading.
- Cross-reference on at least two public sources: Zillow, Redfin, and your county appraiser.

## The Geographic Boundaries That Agents Exploit Most

Three invisible lines move prices more than most buyers realize: school district boundaries, FEMA flood zone boundaries, and HOA lines. A home in a top-rated school district can trade at a 15% to 20% premium over an identical home a block away in a lower-rated district. An agent listing a home just inside the good district boundary will absolutely pull comps from deep inside that district while ignoring the cheaper sales on the other side of the line. Similarly, properties in FEMA Special Flood Hazard Areas — designated Zone AE or Zone VE — carry mandatory flood insurance costs that can add $3,000 to $8,000 per year to ownership costs, which suppresses their market values. If your comp set mixes flood-zone and non-flood-zone properties without adjustment, the numbers are meaningless. Always verify a subject property's flood zone designation at FEMA's Flood Map Service Center before you accept any CMA at face value.

## What a Clean, Unbiased Comp Set Actually Looks Like

A legitimate comp analysis starts with a tight, rules-based filter applied before anyone looks at the dollar figures — not after. The filter should include: sold (not pending, not listed) within the past 90 days; within 0.25 to 0.5 miles of the subject property; within 15% of the subject's square footage; same property type (single-family is not a comp for a condo); similar lot size within 25%; and same flood zone designation. Apply those filters first, then look at what comes out. If fewer than three sales pass, you widen the radius or extend the time window — and you document why, rather than quietly swapping in shinier properties. Every exclusion should be explainable on the record. If you ask an agent for that methodology and they get defensive or vague, you have your answer about how scientific the process really is.

> **A good comp set starts with rules, not results. If the comps were chosen to hit a number rather than reveal one, the whole analysis is backwards.**
