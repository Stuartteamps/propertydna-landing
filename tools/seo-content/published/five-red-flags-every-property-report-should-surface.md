# Five Red Flags Every Property Report Should Surface

A property report should do one thing: tell you the truth about a home before you're legally committed to buying it. Five red flags show up again and again in deals that go sideways — flood zone exposure, unpermitted construction, title clouds, HOA financial distress, and zoning non-conformance. If your report isn't surfacing all five, you're flying blind with a six-figure decision.

Here's the uncomfortable reality: the standard disclosure packet your agent hands you was assembled by the seller. The inspection report was ordered by you but interpreted for you by someone who wants the deal to close. Real property intelligence means pulling raw data — permit records, FEMA maps, lien filings, HOA financials, zoning overlays — and reading what it actually says. That's the difference between a property report and a property DNA report.

## Red Flag 1: Flood Zone Exposure That Isn't Disclosed Upfront

Flood risk is the most underpriced hazard in residential real estate right now. FEMA remapped millions of parcels under its Risk Rating 2.0 framework, and the average flood insurance premium jumped more than 25% nationally in the years following the rollout — with some coastal and low-lying properties seeing increases of 300% or more. A seller is often required to disclose if they've made a flood insurance claim, but they are rarely required to tell you what FEMA flood zone designation your property actually sits in, or what the actuarially correct premium will be once you own it. Your property report should pull the official FIRM panel designation — Zone AE, Zone VE, Zone X — and flag any discrepancy between the current designation and what the listing implies. If that data isn't on page one, the report isn't doing its job.

## Red Flag 2: Unpermitted Construction and Open Permits

Unpermitted work is one of the most common mistakes buyers pay for years after closing. A finished basement, a garage conversion, an added bathroom — if the work wasn't permitted and inspected, it doesn't legally exist. That means your homeowner's insurance may refuse to cover a loss in that space, your lender may call the loan if they discover it, and you will almost certainly have to disclose it when you sell. A thorough property report cross-references the county assessor's square footage and room count against the active permit history. Discrepancies of even 200-300 square feet should trigger a deeper look. Equally dangerous: an open permit from a prior owner's renovation that was never closed out. Open permits can block your own future permits and create title complications. Check the permit log — every single line item.

> **Unpermitted square footage doesn't just lower resale value — it can void your insurance coverage on the exact room where a fire or flood starts.**

## Red Flag 3: Title Clouds — Liens, Judgments, and Easements That Survive Closing

Title insurance exists because title problems are common. What most buyers don't realize is that a title commitment — the document that tells you what the title company will insure — is full of exceptions. Those exceptions are the things the title company will not cover. Mechanic's liens from a contractor the seller never paid, federal tax liens, judgment liens from a divorce or lawsuit, and utility easements that restrict what you can build are all standard exceptions. Your property report should summarize what's on title in plain English before you pay for a full title search, so you know whether to walk, negotiate, or accept the risk. An unresolved mechanic's lien can easily run $15,000 to $80,000 and becomes your problem the moment you take title if it isn't cleared.

## Red Flag 4: HOA Financial Distress and Pending Special Assessments

If you're buying a condo or a home in a planned community, you're not just buying a unit — you're buying a share of an organization. And that organization can be broke. Underfunded HOA reserves are an epidemic right now, accelerated by aging infrastructure and tighter reserve study requirements in many states following high-profile structural incidents. A financially distressed HOA has two options: raise dues or levy a special assessment. Special assessments on condo units have ranged from a few thousand dollars all the way to six figures for major structural remediation. Your property report should flag the HOA's reserve funding ratio — anything below 70% funded is a yellow flag, and below 50% is a serious red flag — along with any pending litigation the association is involved in, which your lender will likely discover anyway and may use to deny your loan.

- Request the last 2 years of HOA meeting minutes — deferred maintenance conversations live there
- Ask for the most recent reserve study, not just the reserve balance
- Check for any active or threatened litigation involving the association
- Verify the monthly dues figure in the listing against the actual HOA billing statement
- Confirm whether the HOA is FHA or VA approved if you need those loan types

## Red Flag 5: Zoning Non-Conformance and Overlay Restrictions

Zoning is the most overlooked dimension of property risk, especially in markets that have updated their codes in the last decade. A non-conforming use means the property was built or used in a way that was legal under old rules but doesn't conform to the current zoning code. This sounds theoretical until you try to rebuild after a fire and discover you can only replace 49% of the structure, or you try to add an ADU and find out the lot coverage limits prohibit it. Overlay districts — historic preservation zones, coastal setback zones, airport noise corridors, wildfire hazard areas — layer additional restrictions on top of base zoning that aren't always visible on the standard listing. A property report should pull the base zoning designation, flag any non-conforming status, and identify overlay districts that could limit your use of the property or its future resale value.

## Why Most Property Reports Miss These Five Flags

Standard disclosure packets are seller-generated documents. Home inspections cover physical conditions on the day of the inspection, not historical records or legal encumbrances. Preliminary title reports are written in legal language designed for attorneys, not buyers under deadline pressure. The gap between what buyers are handed and what they actually need to make an informed decision is where the real estate industry has historically profited.
