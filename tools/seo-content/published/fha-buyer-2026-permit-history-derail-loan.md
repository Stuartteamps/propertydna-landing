# FHA Buyer 2026: What Permit History Can Derail Your Loan

If you're using an FHA loan to buy a home in 2026, unpermitted work is one of the fastest ways to lose your loan at the appraisal stage — sometimes just days before closing. FHA appraisers are required to flag any improvement that appears to have been done without a permit, and when they do, lenders can demand costly remediation, refuse to close, or require you to renegotiate the entire deal. The hard truth: sellers frequently don't disclose unpermitted work, and your buyer's agent — who gets paid only when the deal closes — has limited incentive to dig into the permit record before you fall in love with the house.

The clearest answer: before you make an offer on any property as an FHA buyer, pull the permit history for that address. Look specifically for additions, garage conversions, finished basements, new electrical panels, HVAC replacements, and roof work that have no corresponding closed permit. Even one open or missing permit on a structural improvement can trigger an FHA appraisal condition that stalls or kills your loan. This article walks you through exactly which permit issues are the most dangerous, why the FHA's Minimum Property Standards make this non-negotiable, and how to protect yourself before you're in contract.

## Why FHA Loans Are More Sensitive to Permit Issues Than Conventional Loans

FHA loans are backed by the federal government through HUD, which means HUD sets the rules — and HUD is serious about property condition. Unlike a conventional loan where an appraisal is primarily about value, an FHA appraisal is also a property condition inspection. The appraiser must certify that the home meets FHA Minimum Property Standards (MPS), a set of health and safety requirements that go well beyond what a typical home inspection covers. If the appraiser spots a finished basement, an added bedroom, or an extended roofline with no matching permit in the local record, they are trained to note it. Once it's noted, your lender has to act on it — they don't have discretion.

In 2025, FHA loan originations accounted for roughly 15% of all purchase mortgages nationwide, and first-time buyers represented over 80% of FHA purchase volume. That means millions of buyers who are already stretching to afford a down payment are also the most exposed to permit surprises that could blow up their financing at the worst possible moment.

## The Six Types of Unpermitted Work That Trigger FHA Appraisal Conditions

- Garage conversions to living space: This is the single most common FHA trigger. If the listing shows 3 bedrooms but the original permit record only shows a 2-bedroom home with a garage, an FHA appraiser will flag it. The converted space may lack proper egress windows, insulation, or electrical — all FHA MPS violations.
- Unpermitted room additions: Any square footage added without a permit is officially non-existent to the county and therefore non-existent to FHA. Your appraiser cannot count it in livable square footage, which can drop the appraised value below your purchase price immediately.
- Electrical panel upgrades without a permit: A 200-amp service upgrade sounds like a selling point — unless it was done by a homeowner without pulling a permit and having the work inspected. FHA appraisers are instructed to flag visible electrical work that shows no permit trail.
- Roof replacements with open or expired permits: A seller who replaced a roof three years ago but never had the final inspection signed off has an open permit. FHA lenders treat an open permit as unresolved work — they will require resolution before closing.
- Basement finishing without permits: Finished basements in flood-prone areas are already a dual concern (more on that below), but even in dry markets, a finished basement with no permit means the framing, insulation, and electrical were never inspected.
- Water heater or HVAC replacements without permits: These are considered mechanical systems under FHA MPS. An unpermitted replacement raises questions about whether the installation meets code — a direct health and safety concern under HUD guidelines.
- Deck or fence additions: Structural additions to the exterior that lack permits can become liability issues and are increasingly being flagged by appraisers, especially when the work appears recent.

> **An open permit from work completed five years ago can still kill your FHA loan closing in 2026. The seller's 'we just forgot to close it out' explanation does not move your lender.**

## The Difference Between an Open Permit, a Closed Permit, and No Permit — And Why It Matters

Permit status is not binary. A permit can be pulled, meaning work was planned and registered with the municipality. It can be closed or finaled, meaning a building inspector approved the completed work. Or it can be open — pulled but never inspected to completion. Then there is the worst case: no permit at all, meaning the work was done in secret. For FHA purposes, open permits and no-permit situations are both problems, but they present differently. An open permit suggests the work was started legally but never finished legally. A no-permit situation means there is no government record the work was ever reviewed for safety. Both will show up on an FHA appraisal condition. The resolution for an open permit is sometimes as simple as scheduling the final inspection — if the work was done correctly. The resolution for no-permit work can require hiring a licensed contractor to assess and potentially tear open walls for inspection, costing anywhere from $2,000 to $25,000 or more depending on the scope.

## How to Pull Permit History Before You Make an Offer

Most counties in the United States maintain public permit records, and many have moved these online. The problem is that each county has a different system, different terminology, and different levels of searchability. In some jurisdictions, permits are indexed by parcel number. In others, you search by address. In rural counties, you may have to call the building department directly. The permit record also only tells you what was permitted — it says nothing about work that was done without ever notifying the municipality.

Cross-referencing permit records against listing square footage, tax records, and aerial imagery from different time periods gives you a much stronger picture. If a home's square footage jumped from 1,100 to 1,600 between a 2014 tax record and today's listing, but there is no addition permit in the county record for that period, something was built without documentation. That 500-square-foot discrepancy is exactly the kind of detail that surfaces in an FHA appraisal — and blindsides buyers who only relied on what the listing said.

## The Flood Zone Double Exposure Every FHA Buyer Should Know About

Permit issues and flood zone designation are separate risks, but they compound each other dangerously for FHA buyers. FEMA flood maps designate Special Flood Hazard Areas (SFHAs) — primarily Zone AE and Zone VE — where flood insurance is mandatory for any federally backed mortgage. An FHA loan on a property in Zone AE already requires flood insurance, which can run $2,000 to $8,000 per year in coastal markets. Now add an unpermitted basement conversion to that same property: the appraiser has to flag the unpermitted space, the flood insurance underwriter has to account for the below-grade living area, and suddenly you have a property with two simultaneous closing conditions. Buyers in Florida, Texas, Louisiana, and coastal California need to treat permit history and flood zone designation as a single combined research task, not two separate boxes to check.

## What to Do If You're Already in Contract and Discover a Permit Problem

If you discover a permit issue after you are in contract, you still have options — but the clock matters. First, determine whether your purchase agreement includes an inspection contingency or a financing contingency. In most states, a financing contingency protects you if your lender cannot approve the loan due to property condition, which includes FHA appraisal conditions related to permits. Second, get a written estimate of what resolution would cost. If the seller is unwilling to resolve the permit issue before closing, you can negotiate a price reduction, request a seller credit, or walk away under your contingency without losing your earnest money deposit. Do not let a seller or their agent pressure you into waiving contingencies on a property with known permit problems when you're using FHA financing. That combination is a trap.
