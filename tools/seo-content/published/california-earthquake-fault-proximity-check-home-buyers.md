# California Earthquake Fault Proximity Check for Home Buyers: What You Need to Know Before You Close

If you are buying a home in California, checking earthquake fault proximity is not optional — it is one of the most critical steps in your due diligence. Homes located within a state-designated Alquist-Priolo Earthquake Fault Zone cannot legally be built for human occupancy within 50 feet of an active fault trace, and sellers are required to disclose if a property sits inside one of these zones. But disclosure does not mean the risk disappears, and being just outside the zone boundary does not mean you are safe. Thousands of California properties sit within a few hundred feet of active faults and carry meaningful seismic risk that never makes it into a standard listing conversation.

## What Is an Alquist-Priolo Earthquake Fault Zone and Why Does It Matter?

California passed the Alquist-Priolo Earthquake Fault Zone Act in 1972, directly after the 1971 Sylmar earthquake killed 65 people and caused over $500 million in damage. The law requires the California Geological Survey to map active fault traces and establish 50-foot regulatory zones around them. Any property inside those zones must be disclosed to buyers, and new construction for human habitation is prohibited within the fault trace setback. There are currently more than 500 miles of mapped fault zones across California, covering portions of Los Angeles, the Bay Area, Sacramento, and dozens of suburban communities that buyers assume are low-risk.

> **Being outside an Alquist-Priolo Zone does not mean being outside earthquake risk. Shaking, liquefaction, and landslide hazards extend far beyond the 50-foot fault setback line.**

## The Three Seismic Hazards Buyers Confuse With Each Other

Fault proximity is just one layer of earthquake risk. Buyers who fixate only on the Alquist-Priolo designation miss two other hazards that can be equally destructive and are mapped separately by the state.

- Fault Rupture Zones (Alquist-Priolo): The ground literally tears apart along the fault trace. This is the risk the 50-foot setback rule addresses. Properties directly on the fault line face structural destruction that no retrofit can fully prevent.
- Liquefaction Zones: Loose, water-saturated soils behave like liquid during strong shaking. Entire foundations can sink or tilt. Large portions of the San Francisco Bay Area, Sacramento Delta, and coastal Southern California flatlands sit in mapped liquefaction hazard zones — sometimes miles from the nearest fault.
- Earthquake-Induced Landslide Zones: Hillside properties in areas like the Hollywood Hills, Oakland Hills, and Palos Verdes Peninsula face sliding and slope failure risk during major seismic events. The CGS maps these separately, and many buyers in scenic hillside communities have no idea their lot is inside one.

## What California Law Requires Sellers to Disclose

California has some of the strongest natural hazard disclosure laws in the country, but they are narrower than most buyers assume. Sellers are legally required to provide a Natural Hazard Disclosure report — commonly called an NHD — that flags whether the property sits inside six state-designated hazard zones, including Alquist-Priolo fault zones and seismic hazard zones for liquefaction and landslide. What the law does not require is any contextual explanation of what those designations mean, how close the nearest active fault actually is, what the estimated peak ground acceleration is for the site, or what earthquake-specific insurance would cost. Sellers hand buyers a checkbox form, buyers sign it, and the conversation ends. That is the gap PropertyDNA was built to close.

2. Cross-reference with the CGS Fault Activity Map of California, available at the California Geological Survey website. This shows the mapped fault traces you can zoom in on to measure approximate distance from the property.
3. Check the USGS Unified Hazard Tool for peak ground acceleration (PGA) at the site. A PGA of 0.5g or higher at 2% probability of exceedance in 50 years puts a property in the highest shaking risk tier — this threshold is exceeded across much of coastal California.
4. Search the county assessor and building permit records for any prior retrofit work, foundation replacement, or geotechnical study on the property. Sellers are not required to volunteer this history, but it is often in the public record.
5. Ask your own inspector — not the seller's — to evaluate the structure for seismic retrofit status, including cripple wall bracing, anchor bolts, and soft-story conditions if the home is multi-story.

## What Fault Proximity Actually Does to Home Values and Insurance

Academic research from the University of Southern California found that homes within Alquist-Priolo zones sold at discounts ranging from 3% to 8% compared to similar homes just outside the zone boundary — but only in markets where buyers were informed about the designation. In lower-information markets, the discount largely disappeared, meaning buyers paid full price for fault-zone risk they did not know they were accepting. The insurance side is more stark: standard homeowners policies in California explicitly exclude earthquake damage, and a separate California Earthquake Authority policy for a wood-frame home in a high-hazard zone can run $2,000 to $6,000 or more per year depending on construction type, distance to fault, and soil conditions. That annual premium adds up to real money over a 30-year hold and should be factored into your affordability calculation before you bid.

> **Earthquake insurance is not included in your homeowners policy. In high-hazard California zones, that separate policy can cost $4,000 or more per year — a number your lender will never mention at pre-approval.**

## Red Flags That Suggest Higher Fault Risk in a Listing

Most of these signals appear in plain sight if you know what to look for during a property search and tour.

- The listing notes 'AS-IS sale' or 'seller makes no representations about property condition' — common language when sellers want to limit liability around known disclosures.
- The NHD report has an Alquist-Priolo checkbox marked 'Yes' but no supplemental explanation is offered and your agent moves past it quickly.
- Cracks in the foundation, driveway, or exterior stucco that run diagonally — these can indicate differential settling from soil movement.
- The property sits on alluvial flatland near a river, bay, or filled land area — prime liquefaction territory even if it appears far from a mapped fault.
- No record of seismic retrofit permits when the home is pre-1980 wood frame construction — older cripple wall homes are among the most vulnerable building types in California.
- The listing is priced meaningfully below neighborhood comps with no obvious cosmetic reason — sometimes the seller is quietly pricing in known risk.
