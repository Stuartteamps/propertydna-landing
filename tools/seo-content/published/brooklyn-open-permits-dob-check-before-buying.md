# Brooklyn Open Permits: How to Do a DOB Check Before You Buy

Before you wire a single dollar toward a Brooklyn brownstone or condo, you need to run a New York City Department of Buildings check. Open permits — meaning construction or renovation work that was filed but never formally closed out — attach to the property, not the owner who pulled them. That means if the seller's contractor opened a permit in 2017 to gut-renovate a kitchen and never got a sign-off, that liability is yours the moment you close. It can block refinancing, void title insurance, and cost you thousands to resolve.

The short answer: go to the NYC DOB Buildings Information System (BIS) at nyc.gov/buildings, type in the Brooklyn address, and look for any jobs with a status of 'Open' or 'Partially Approved.' Anything that isn't 'Signed Off' or 'Completed' is a red flag you need to investigate before contract. ## Why Open Permits Are a Bigger Problem in Brooklyn Than Almost Anywhere Else

Brooklyn's housing stock is old. Brownstones in Park Slope, Crown Heights, and Bed-Stuy routinely date back to the late 1800s, and most have been renovated multiple times by multiple owners — often by contractors who pulled permits, did the work, and never bothered to schedule a final inspection. According to NYC DOB data, tens of thousands of construction jobs across the five boroughs remain open for five years or more. Brooklyn, with its dense concentration of pre-war attached houses and multi-family buildings, accounts for a disproportionate share. A single block in Bushwick or Flatbush can have a dozen properties with unresolved permits from renovations done in the 2000s and 2010s.

> **Open permits attach to the property, not the previous owner. If you close without resolving them, you inherit the liability — full stop.**

## How to Run a DOB Check on a Brooklyn Address Right Now

1. Go to nyc.gov/buildings and click 'Look Up Property.'
2. Enter the Brooklyn address. Note: you may need the borough (Brooklyn = BK), block, and lot number if the street address returns multiple results. You can find the BBL (Borough-Block-Lot) on the NYC Finance property portal.
3. Click on 'Jobs/Filings' to see every construction job ever filed on the property.
4. Filter for job status. You want 'Signed Off' on everything. 'Open,' 'Partially Approved,' 'Disapproved,' or 'Suspended' are all problems.
5. Check the 'Violations' tab separately. DOB violations and ECB (Environmental Control Board) violations are different buckets — both can cost money to resolve.
6. Look at the Certificate of Occupancy (CO). If a major renovation was done and the CO was never updated, the legal use of the property may not match what you're buying.
7. Download or screenshot everything. Your attorney needs this.

## What Types of Open Permits Show Up Most Often in Brooklyn

Not all open permits carry the same risk. A permit for a minor plumbing repair that was never closed out is annoying but usually resolvable for a few hundred dollars with a licensed plumber who can schedule a final inspection. The ones that should stop you cold are different.

- Structural work permits (Alt-1 or Alt-2 filings): These cover changes to load-bearing walls, floor plans, or building systems. If a seller removed a wall to create an open-plan living room and never got sign-off, you may be living with an illegal structural modification.
- Illegal conversion permits: Many Brooklyn two-families were illegally converted to three- or four-families. If the DOB ever opened a job on that work and it was never resolved, you could face enforcement action.
- Facade and fire-escape violations: Common in older Brooklyn row houses, these are safety-related and the city can issue vacate orders.
- Elevator filings: Relevant for brownstones converted to condos — an open elevator permit can freeze building operations.
- Cellar or basement apartment work: A converted basement unit with an open permit often signals the unit isn't legal for occupancy, which affects rental income calculations if you're buying as an investment.

## How Open Permits Can Kill Your Mortgage or Title Insurance

Your lender's appraiser is required to flag open permits. If a significant open permit shows up during the appraisal process, many lenders will condition the loan on resolution before closing — or decline entirely. Title insurance companies, which you need to protect your ownership interest, will often exclude coverage for any losses related to open permits unless they're resolved or insured around separately. Some title underwriters in New York will issue what's called an 'open permit endorsement,' but that endorsement typically costs extra and doesn't fully protect you if the city later requires expensive remediation. The cleanest path is always permit resolution before you close, with the cost negotiated as a seller credit or price reduction. A seller credit for permit resolution in Brooklyn can range from $1,500 for a simple plumbing sign-off to well over $25,000 for a structural or conversion issue that requires hiring an architect and a licensed expediter.

## What to Ask For in the Contract If You Find Open Permits

This is where your attorney earns their fee. If your DOB check surfaces open permits, you have several contract options depending on how serious the permits are and how motivated the seller is.

- Seller closes the permit before closing: Ideal. The seller hires a licensed professional, schedules the inspection, gets the sign-off, and proves it before you wire funds. Build in a deadline and a right to cancel if it's not done.
- Purchase price reduction: You accept the permit as-is but negotiate a dollar amount off the price that covers your estimated resolution cost — plus a buffer for surprises.
- Escrow holdback: A specific dollar amount (often 150% of the estimated resolution cost) is held in escrow by the attorney and released to the seller only after you confirm the permit is closed post-closing.
- Title insurance endorsement: Your title company insures over the open permit for a premium. Use this only for minor, low-risk permits — not structural or conversion issues.
- Walk away: If the seller won't negotiate and the permit is serious, exercising your right to cancel during due diligence is a legitimate outcome. Not every deal is worth closing.

## Don't Rely on Your Agent or Attorney to Catch This Automatically

Here's the uncomfortable truth: your buyer's agent is focused on getting the deal done. Their commission depends on it. Many real estate attorneys in New York do a title search but don't always do a deep-dive DOB review as part of their standard engagement — especially if you're not asking for it explicitly. That's not necessarily negligence; it's the normal division of labor in a transaction. But it means the burden is on you to either check the DOB yourself or use a platform built to surface exactly this kind of risk. A title search will catch recorded liens and judgments, but it won't always catch an open permit filed in 2009 by a contractor who's since gone out of business. You have to go to the DOB directly — or use a tool that does it for you.
