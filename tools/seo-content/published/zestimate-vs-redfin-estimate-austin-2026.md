# Zestimate vs Redfin Estimate in Austin 2026: Which One Should You Actually Trust?

In Austin's volatile housing market, both the Zestimate and Redfin Estimate can miss a home's real value by 5% to 10% or more — and in some zip codes closer to East Austin or the Domain, that gap can represent $40,000 to $80,000 on a median-priced home. Neither tool is designed to protect you as a buyer. They are lead-generation products built to get you to contact an agent. If you need a number you can negotiate with, you need something built differently.

The short answer: neither Zillow nor Redfin publishes Austin-specific median error rates in real time, but Zillow's own national off-market median absolute percentage error (MAPE) has historically hovered around 6.9% to 7.5%, while Redfin has claimed figures closer to 6.2% for on-market homes. ## How Zestimate and Redfin Estimate Actually Work

Both tools are automated valuation models, or AVMs. They ingest public records — deed transfers, tax assessments, square footage, lot size, number of bedrooms — and run that data through proprietary machine-learning models to spit out a price estimate. Zillow also layers in user-submitted data and listing photos. Redfin feeds its model with its own brokerage transaction data. Neither tool has walked through your target property. Neither knows the kitchen was last renovated in 2007, that the HVAC is on its last legs, or that the home backs up to a drainage easement that floods every spring. They are working from a spreadsheet, not a walkthrough.

## Why Austin Is an Especially Dangerous City to Trust AVMs

Austin is an AVM graveyard. The city saw one of the steepest appreciation runs in U.S. history between 2020 and 2022 — median prices climbed nearly 60% in under 24 months in some submarkets like 78704 and 78745. Then came the correction. By late 2023 and into 2024, certain neighborhoods had given back 20% or more of those gains. AVM models train on trailing comparable sales, which means they are always chasing the market, never leading it. In a city that swings this hard, the model's training data can be six to twelve months behind actual market sentiment. That lag is money out of your pocket.

- New construction communities like Mueller, East Riverside, and parts of Leander skew comparables because builder incentives — rate buydowns, closing cost credits — rarely appear in public records the way a price reduction would.
- Austin has thousands of short-term rental conversions that affect neighborhood price dynamics but are invisible to standard AVM inputs.
- The Travis Central Appraisal District caps assessed value increases at 10% per year for homesteaded properties, creating a massive gap between assessed value and market value that confuses models trained on tax records.
- Flood zone designations from FEMA — including Zone AE and Zone X designations that differ block by block in Austin's creek-heavy terrain — can swing a property's real value significantly but are not weighted properly in either AVM.

## Zestimate vs Redfin Estimate: A Head-to-Head Breakdown

For on-market homes — where the listing price itself becomes a massive data input — accuracy improves to roughly 2.4%. That sounds good until you realize that by the time you are shopping a listing, you already know the list price. The Zestimate is most useful when you are researching an off-market property, and that is exactly when it is least accurate. Redfin's Estimate is built on a similar framework but benefits from Redfin's brokerage transaction data, which includes more granular MLS fields. Redfin claims a national median error rate of approximately 6.2% for off-market homes, a marginal edge over Zillow. In practice, for an Austin home priced around $550,000 — close to the 2025 Austin median — a 6.5% error means the estimate could be off by $35,750 in either direction. That is not a rounding error. Here is where both AVMs fall apart in a city like Austin. The City of Austin Building Services issues tens of thousands of residential permits annually. Others reveal deferred problems — foundation repairs, unpermitted work being corrected after the fact. Neither Zillow nor Redfin surfaces permit history in their estimate methodology in any meaningful way. An Austin home that had 14 open or closed permits over a five-year span could be a renovated gem or a money pit under litigation. The Zestimate will not tell you which.
