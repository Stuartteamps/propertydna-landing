# Home Buyer Due Diligence Checklist 2026: What to Verify Before Signing

Before you sign a purchase contract in 2026, there are at least a dozen things about that property that the seller is not required to volunteer — and that your agent, who earns a commission only if the deal closes, may not have time to dig into. The short answer: verify title, permits, flood designation, HOA financials, insurance history, zoning, liens, and the physical condition of the home independently, before your due diligence period expires. Miss even one of these and you could be inheriting someone else's $40,000 problem the day you get the keys.

This checklist is built for real buyers in a market where inventory is still tight, seller disclosures vary wildly by state, and AI-powered property intelligence tools now make institutional-level research available to anyone with a phone. Use every tool available to you. The goal is simple: know more about the property than the person selling it.

## 1. Pull a Full Property History Report — Day One

The first thing you do after going under contract is not schedule the inspector. It is pulling a comprehensive property history report. You want to see every ownership transfer, every recorded lien, any foreclosure activity, prior sale prices, and days-on-market patterns going back at least ten years. A house that sold three times in five years is telling you something. A property that was listed, went pending, then came back to market twice in 18 months is telling you something louder. ## 2. Verify Every Permit — Including the Ones That Were Never Pulled

Unpermitted work is one of the most common and costly surprises buyers face. Nationally, estimates suggest that roughly 1 in 5 homes has at least one improvement — a finished basement, a converted garage, an added bathroom, a deck — that was done without a required permit. This matters for three reasons: your lender may not finance a property with significant unpermitted square footage, your insurance carrier may deny claims related to unpermitted structures, and the municipality can require you to tear out the work entirely. Check your county's public permit portal directly, and compare it against the listing's stated square footage and room count. Any discrepancy is a red flag worth a written explanation from the seller.

> **Roughly 1 in 5 homes has at least one unpermitted improvement. You won't find it in the listing description.**

## 3. Check the Flood Zone Designation and Insurance Cost Before You Fall in Love

FEMA flood map designations changed significantly after the introduction of Risk Rating 2.0, which went fully into effect for all National Flood Insurance Program policies in April 2022. Under the old system, a property in a moderate-risk zone might have carried an annual flood premium of $700. Under Risk Rating 2.0, that same property could be rated at $2,400 or more per year based on actual risk factors like first-floor elevation, distance to water, and property value. Before you make an offer, search the address on FEMA's Flood Map Service Center and get an actual insurance quote — not an estimate. In some coastal Florida and Louisiana markets, buyers have been blindsided by flood premiums that added more than $300 per month to their total housing cost.

## 4. Audit the HOA — Its Financials, Its Rules, and Its Litigation History

If the property is in a homeowners association, condo association, or co-op, you are not just buying a home. You are buying into a small government. Request the last three years of HOA meeting minutes, the most recent reserve fund study, the current balance sheet, and any pending special assessments. A healthy HOA should have its reserve fund funded to at least 70 percent of its recommended level — many are far below that. A reserve fund that is only 30 percent funded in a community with aging infrastructure means a special assessment is coming, and it could be $10,000 or $25,000 or more per unit. Also check whether the association is currently party to any litigation. In Florida, condo associations facing Milestone Inspection requirements and structural reserve mandates under SB 4-D are triggering assessments that are reshaping the condo market entirely.

- Request three years of HOA board meeting minutes — look for recurring complaints, deferred maintenance, or budget overruns
- Ask for the most recent reserve fund study and the current reserve fund balance
- Confirm whether any special assessments are pending or have been approved but not yet collected
- Check the HOA's litigation history through your title company or county court records
- Read the CC&Rs cover to cover — short-term rental restrictions, pet policies, and parking rules can be deal-breakers
- Verify HOA dues have not increased more than 15 percent annually in recent years, a signal of financial stress

## 5. Order a Title Search and Understand What Title Insurance Actually Covers

A title search examines the chain of ownership going back decades to surface any clouds on title — recorded liens, unpaid contractor judgments, IRS tax liens, estate disputes, easements, or boundary encroachments that could affect your right to the property. Do not skip owner's title insurance because it seems like an unnecessary closing cost. A lender's policy, which you are almost certainly required to buy, protects only the bank. An owner's policy, which typically costs between $500 and $2,500 depending on purchase price and state, protects you against defects that show up after closing. Mechanic's liens, for example, can attach to a property after you buy it if a contractor the seller hired was never paid. Title insurance is the one closing cost that is genuinely worth every dollar.

## 6. Go Beyond the Standard Home Inspection

A general home inspection covers the visible and accessible. It does not cover what is hidden, what is specialized, or what requires a licensed specialist to assess. Depending on the property, your due diligence period should include a sewer scope inspection for homes over 20 years old (a collapsed or root-invaded lateral line costs $8,000 to $15,000 to replace), a radon test in states where radon is prevalent, a mold assessment if there is any history of water intrusion or the inspector notes staining, a structural engineering review if the inspector flags foundation concerns, and a roof certification if the roof is more than 10 years old. In markets where wood-destroying organisms are a concern — most of the Southeast and Pacific Coast — a separate termite and pest inspection is non-negotiable. Budget $1,500 to $3,000 for the full inspection suite. It is the cheapest insurance you will ever buy.

## 7. Verify Zoning, Land Use, and Any Proposed Nearby Development

Zoning determines what can be built next to you, what you can do with your own property, and — increasingly important — whether short-term rental income is legal. Check the parcel's zoning designation with the county or city planning department, and ask specifically whether there are any pending rezoning applications for nearby parcels. A parcel currently zoned agricultural that is being considered for a distribution warehouse is a fact the seller may not know and is certainly not required to disclose. Also look at the county's capital improvements plan to see whether any major infrastructure projects — highways, transit corridors, industrial parks — are planned within a mile of the property. This information is public. Most buyers never look at it.

## Your Due Diligence Period Is a Deadline — Treat It Like One

Most purchase contracts give you 10 to 15 days for due diligence. Some competitive markets have compressed this to 7 days or less. That window is not administrative paperwork time — it is your only contractual opportunity to walk away with your earnest money intact. Build a day-by-day timeline from the moment you go under contract: day one for pulling property history and ordering the title search, days two and three for the inspection suite, days four through seven for reviewing HOA documents and flood insurance quotes, days eight through ten for any specialist inspections or permit verification follow-up.
