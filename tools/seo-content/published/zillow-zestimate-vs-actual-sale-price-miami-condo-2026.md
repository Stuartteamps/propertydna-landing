# Zillow vs Actual Sale Price: How Accurate Are Zestimates for Miami Condos in 2026?

Zillow's Zestimate is off by a median of 6.6% for off-market properties nationwide — but in Miami's condo market, that error rate routinely balloons to 10–15% or higher. For a $650,000 Brickell condo, that's a $65,000–$97,500 gap between what Zillow shows and what the unit actually closes for. If you're using a Zestimate to decide whether a Miami condo is priced fairly in 2026, you are flying blind with a borrowed compass.

The core problem is structural: Zillow's algorithm was built on single-family home data, and Miami condos break nearly every assumption it makes. Special assessments, building reserve deficiencies, short-term rental restrictions, association litigation, and floor-level premiums are invisible to the Zestimate model. The result is an estimate that looks precise but is systematically misleading in a market where those factors regularly swing value by six figures.

## Why the Zestimate Fails Specifically in Miami

Miami is one of the most condo-dense cities in the United States, and its condo market is layered with variables that automated valuation models simply cannot ingest from public MLS records alone. To your bank account, they are worlds apart.

- Floor-level premiums: Units on floors 20+ can command 15–25% more than identical units on floor 5, a pattern Zestimates frequently average away.
- Special assessments: Post-Surfside structural inspections have triggered assessments ranging from $8,000 to over $150,000 per unit in some older Miami Beach buildings.
- Rental restrictions: Buildings that restrict short-term rentals lose significant buyer pools, suppressing real prices in ways the algorithm misses.

- Deferred reserves: Florida's new reserve-funding mandates mean buildings with under-funded reserves face compulsory catch-up costs passed directly to owners.

## Zestimate vs. Appraisal vs. Actual Sale: The Three Numbers That Matter

Buyers often conflate three very different numbers: the Zestimate, the bank appraisal, and the actual contract price. Understanding how they relate is critical in Miami's 2026 condo market. The Zestimate is a marketing tool — Zillow's business model is selling leads to agents, not protecting your equity. A bank appraisal is a lender's risk-management document; it is backward-looking, based on closed comps, and often lags a fast-moving market by 60–90 days. The actual contract price is the only number that reflects current, real-world demand — and in Miami, that number has been increasingly driven by cash buyers, foreign capital, and inventory pressure that no algorithm can fully model in real time. In Q1 2025, approximately 43% of Miami-area condo transactions were all-cash, according to Miami Realtors data. Cash buyers do not need appraisals, which means a significant portion of the market closes at prices that are never stress-tested by a lender's valuation model — and that Zestimates consistently trail.
