## Permit History: The Hidden Variable in Every Trousdale Transaction

The Beverly Hills Building and Safety Division maintains records on every permitted improvement to every parcel in the city. In a neighborhood like Trousdale, where homes have been bought, renovated, flipped, and expanded repeatedly over six decades, a single address can accumulate dozens of permits — some finaled correctly, some expired, and some pulled but never inspected. An open permit is not just a bureaucratic nuisance. It can signal unpermitted structural work, a pool that was never signed off, a guesthouse addition that does not comply with current setback rules, or a grading modification that was never certified by a geotechnical engineer. Any of these can cloud title, trigger mandatory remediation at your expense post-close, or create issues when you eventually try to sell. One pattern that shows up consistently in Trousdale: pools and spa additions permitted in the 1970s and 1980s that were never formally finaled, sometimes because the original owners simply never called for a final inspection. The permit sat open for forty years. Now it is your problem.

- Pull the full permit history from Beverly Hills Building and Safety before making an offer — not after.
- Look for permits issued but never finaled, especially for pools, grading, retaining walls, and additions.
- Ask the seller's agent for the most recent geotechnical report on the property. If they do not have one, that tells you something.
- Verify that any guesthouse or ADU on the property was permitted under current Beverly Hills zoning rules, not grandfathered under a prior regime that no longer applies.
- Confirm that all retaining walls have engineered drawings on file and were signed off by a licensed structural engineer.
