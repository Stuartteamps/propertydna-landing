# What to Do When Your DNA Score Is Below 70

If your PropertyDNA score comes back below 70, stop. Do not schedule a second showing. Do not write an offer. A score under 70 means the platform has detected one or more meaningful risk signals attached to that address — and you deserve to understand exactly what they are before you put a single dollar on the table. A low score is not automatically a deal-breaker, but it is a mandatory conversation starter.

The good news: knowing your score is below 70 already puts you miles ahead of the average buyer, who typically discovers problems after closing — if they discover them at all. Most buyers get a four-page inspection report and a handshake. You have institutional-grade data. Now here is what to do with it.

## Understand What the Score Is Actually Measuring

PropertyDNA scores are built from dozens of layered data signals: permit history, flood zone designations, lien records, HOA health, code violations, insurance claim history, environmental proximity, and more. A score below 70 does not mean the house is ugly or overpriced — it means the data has flagged something structural, legal, financial, or environmental that statistically increases buyer risk. One property might score 62 because of three open permits pulled in the last 18 months and no certificate of occupancy issued. Another scores 58 because it sits inside FEMA Flood Zone AE, where mandatory flood insurance can add $3,000 to $8,000 per year to your carrying cost. The number is a composite. Your first job is to read the breakdown, not just the headline score.

## Pull the Full Report and Identify the Specific Flags

Every PropertyDNA report organizes risk into categories. When your score is below 70, open the report and look for which category is dragging the number down. The most common culprits buyers miss fall into a predictable set of buckets.

- Open or unpermitted work: Renovations completed without a permit can become your liability the moment you take title. Lenders increasingly refuse to finance these properties.
- Flood or climate risk: A FEMA Zone AE or Zone VE designation means federal flood insurance is not optional — and premiums have surged more than 40% in some coastal markets since FEMA's Risk Rating 2.0 overhaul.
- Lien history: Mechanic's liens, HOA liens, or IRS tax liens can survive a sale and attach to you as the new owner if not properly cleared at closing.
- Code violations or municipal citations: Some violations are cosmetic. Others — structural, electrical, or habitability-related — require remediation before a lender will fund the loan.
- HOA financial distress signals: If the report flags reserve fund shortfalls or delinquency rates above 15%, you could be walking into a special assessment the seller never disclosed.
- Insurance claims history: Multiple water or fire claims on a property can make it uninsurable — or push premiums so high the monthly carrying cost blows your budget entirely.

> **A score below 70 is the data telling you: someone should have asked these questions already, and nobody did. Now it's your turn.**

## Common Mistakes Buyers Make When They See a Low Score

Here is where most buyers go wrong — and where agents with a commission on the line are least likely to steer you right.

1. Ignoring it because they love the house. Emotional attachment is the single biggest financial liability in real estate. The score does not care about the kitchen backsplash.
2. Assuming the seller's disclosure covers everything. Seller disclosures are legally required in most states, but they only capture what the seller knows and chooses to admit. Permits, liens, and flood designations exist in public records the seller has zero incentive to volunteer.
3. Relying solely on a standard home inspection. A home inspector walks the property once. They do not pull permit histories, check lien records, or assess HOA financials. That is a completely different layer of due diligence.
4. Asking their buyer's agent to 'look into it.' Your agent's commission is contingent on this deal closing. That is not a conspiracy — it is a structural conflict of interest. Get independent data.
5. Negotiating price without understanding the true cost of the flags. A $20,000 price reduction sounds like a win until you learn the open-permit issue requires $35,000 in remediation to satisfy the lender.
6. Skipping title insurance or skimping on the title search. A thorough title search costs a few hundred dollars. An undiscovered lien after closing can cost you tens of thousands.

## How to Use a Low Score as a Negotiating Tool

Here is the flip side: a below-70 score is leverage if you know how to use it. Most buyers walk away from a flagged property. That means less competition, and that means you have negotiating room the seller has not been forced to acknowledge yet. Once you have identified the specific flags from your report, quantify the cost to cure each one. Get a permit expediter to estimate the cost and timeline to close out open permits. Get an insurance quote that reflects the actual flood zone designation. Call an HOA attorney if the reserve fund numbers look thin. Then go back to the seller with a documented ask: either cure the issues before closing, or reduce the price by the verified cost to cure. Sellers who have been sitting on a flagged property — and many do not even know they have one — are often more motivated than they let on.

## When a Low Score Should Be an Absolute Walk-Away

Not every low-score property is salvageable, and buyers who convince themselves otherwise often pay the steepest price. There are specific flag combinations where walking away is the only rational move. If a property scores below 60 and carries simultaneous flags for active code violations, an unresolved mechanic's lien, and a flood zone AE designation with no existing elevation certificate on file, you are looking at a situation where closing is genuinely uncertain and post-closing costs are genuinely unknowable. Similarly, if the HOA has pending litigation — which sometimes appears as a flag in financial or legal data layers — lenders may refuse to finance units in that community entirely, making resale nearly impossible. Properties with documented environmental contamination flags, particularly those within 500 feet of a former dry cleaner, gas station, or industrial site, may require Phase I and Phase II environmental assessments before a bank will touch them. At some point, the opportunity cost of spending weeks and thousands in due diligence on a property that may never close is itself a reason to move on.

> **Your agent gets paid when you close. PropertyDNA gets paid when you're protected. Those are not the same incentive.**

## Your Step-by-Step Action Plan for a Sub-70 Score

- Step 1: Read the full report breakdown — not just the headline number. Identify which specific categories are flagged and at what severity.
- Step 2: Prioritize flags by financial impact. Flood zone costs, lien amounts, and permit remediation estimates are the big three. Price discovery on each one before you proceed.
- Step 3: Order an independent title search if you haven't already. Do not rely on the seller's title company — hire your own.
- Step 4: Get lender pre-approval with the specific flags disclosed. Some issues will kill financing before you waste months in escrow.
- Step 5: If you still want the property, use the documented flags to negotiate. Request seller credits, price reductions, or escrow holdbacks tied to specific remediation milestones.
- Step 6: If the flags cannot be quantified or cured within your timeline and budget, walk. There is no shame in using data to make a rational decision.
- Step 7: Run a DNA report on your backup property before you fall in love with that one too.
