## What Makes Movie Colony Different From Every Other Palm Springs Neighborhood

Movie Colony is not a marketing name — it is a formally designated neighborhood bounded roughly by Alejo Road to the north, Indian Canyon Drive to the west, Ramon Road to the south, and Sunrise Way to the east. The neighborhood gained its name organically in the 1920s and 1930s when Hollywood royalty built private retreats here, and that legacy has a direct legal consequence today: a significant portion of the area falls under Palm Springs' Class 1 Historic Site and Section 14 overlay frameworks. That means even a cosmetic renovation — new windows, a garage door, a front wall — can require approval from the city's Architectural Review Committee and, in some cases, the Historic Site Preservation Board. Buyers who plan to personalize their purchase need to budget time, not just money, for that process.

## The Indian Land Lease Question Every Buyer Must Ask First

Palm Springs is built partly on Section 14 allotted land held in trust by the federal government for members of the Agua Caliente Band of Cahuilla Indians. The checkerboard pattern of fee-simple versus leasehold parcels runs through Movie Colony, and the two ownership types behave completely differently in the market. On a leasehold parcel, you own the structure — the house, the pool, the landscaping — but you lease the land beneath it, typically from individual Agua Caliente allottees or the Tribe itself. Lease terms in Movie Colony currently run between 2040 and 2069 expiration dates depending on the parcel. A lease expiring in 2041 is dramatically less bankable than one expiring in 2065, and most lenders apply strict underwriting criteria: they require at least 30 to 35 years of remaining lease term beyond the loan maturity date. If the math doesn't work, your financing doesn't work. Pull the lease expiration date on every leasehold parcel before you waste a weekend at an open house.

- Ask the listing agent directly: fee-simple or leasehold? Get it in writing.
- Request the lease documents and confirm the expiration date — not the most recent renewal option, the actual current term.
- Verify whether lease payments are fixed or CPI-adjusted; many Movie Colony leases include periodic rent resets that can jump significantly.
- Confirm that your lender will underwrite leasehold property in Palm Springs — not all will, even at jumbo loan levels.
- Factor lease payments into your true monthly cost of ownership; they frequently range from $400 to over $1,200 per month on desirable parcels.

## Flood Zone Exposure in Movie Colony: What FEMA Panel Data Actually Says

The Coachella Valley sits in a desert basin, and flash flooding is a legitimate risk that listing photos of sun-drenched courtyards do not convey. A meaningful portion of Movie Colony parcels fall within FEMA-designated Special Flood Hazard Areas — Zone AE or Zone X-shaded — based on Flood Insurance Rate Map panels associated with the Whitewater River system and its tributaries. Zone AE designation triggers mandatory flood insurance requirements for any federally backed mortgage. Even Zone X-shaded properties, which carry moderate risk, are increasingly drawing elevated premiums under FEMA's Risk Rating 2.0 methodology, which took full effect in 2023 and recalculates premiums based on property-specific exposure rather than zone-wide averages. A buyer purchasing a Movie Colony home at $1.85 million who discovers post-closing that their annual flood insurance runs $14,000 — not the $2,200 the prior owner paid under a grandfathered pre-2023 rate — is going to have a very bad year. Check the FEMA panel number, the zone designation, and whether any existing flood policy is assignable before you make an offer.
