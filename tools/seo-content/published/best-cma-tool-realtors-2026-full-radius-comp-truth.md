# Best CMA Tool for Realtors 2026: Full-Radius Comp Truth

The best CMA tool for realtors in 2026 is the one that pulls full-radius comparable sales without cherry-picking — and most of the big names still fail that basic test. If your CMA software only surfaces comps that make the list price look good, it is not a CMA tool. It is a marketing document generator. Realtors who want to actually protect their buyers — and their own liability — need a platform that shows every qualifying sale within a defined radius, flags outliers, and layers in permit history, flood zone data, and days-on-market trends before a single number gets presented.

For buyers reading this: your agent's CMA is only as honest as the tool behind it. The average AVM (automated valuation model) carries a median error rate of 6 to 7 percent on any given property — on a $500,000 home, that is a $30,000 to $35,000 blind spot. The tools reviewed below are ranked on one standard: do they show you the full picture, or do they show you the picture that closes the deal?

## What Makes a CMA Tool Actually Accurate in 2026

Accuracy in a CMA tool comes down to five unglamorous factors: data recency, comp radius flexibility, square-footage tolerance settings, adjustment transparency, and off-MLS sale inclusion. Most legacy platforms — Cloud CMA, RPR, Realist — default to a 0.5-mile radius and a 90-day lookback. That sounds tight and scientific, but in low-turnover neighborhoods it produces three comps when there should be twelve, and in high-turnover markets it swamps the analysis with properties that have nothing in common with the subject. A good tool lets you set a 1-mile or even a 1.5-mile radius and then filter aggressively by property characteristics rather than just geography. The radius should be your starting point, not your ceiling.

> **A CMA that only shows comps supporting the list price is not analysis. It is advocacy — and you are not the client it is advocating for.**

## Why Comp Radius Is the Number One Manipulation Vector

Here is how the game is played: an agent inputs a subject property and the CMA software returns every sale within 0.25 miles in the last 60 days. In a dense urban neighborhood, that might be eight comps, all of them high because the block has been on a run. Expand to 0.75 miles and pull 180 days and you will find twelve more sales, three of which are 10 percent lower and one of which is a distressed sale at 18 percent below the current list price. The tight-radius CMA conveniently never surfaces those. This is not always intentional — the default settings in most CMA platforms are just not built for buyer protection. They are built for speed and for producing a number that does not create friction in the deal. Full-radius comp truth means you set the radius wide, pull the full dataset, and then apply quality filters — not the other way around.

## What Permit History and Flood Zone Data Have to Do With Your CMA

A comp is only a good comp if the properties are actually comparable. A 1,800-square-foot home with an unpermitted 400-square-foot addition is not comparable to a 1,800-square-foot home where every renovation was permitted and inspected — but most CMA tools treat them identically because they are both pulling from the same MLS field. Permit history tells you whether the square footage is real and insurable. Flood zone designation — specifically FEMA Zone AE versus Zone X — can affect insurance costs by $3,000 to $8,000 annually and cap resale value in ways that a pure price-per-square-foot comp will never reveal. Any CMA tool that does not surface these layers is giving you half the picture. In Florida and coastal California markets especially, a Zone AE designation on a comp should automatically trigger a price adjustment, and almost no standard CMA platforms make that adjustment automatically.
