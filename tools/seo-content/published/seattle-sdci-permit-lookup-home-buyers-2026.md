# Seattle SDCI Permit Lookup for Home Buyers: What You Need to Know in 2026

Before you make an offer on any Seattle home, you need to run a permit check through the Seattle Department of Construction and Inspections — known as the SDCI. This is free, takes about five minutes, and can save you from inheriting tens of thousands of dollars in unpermitted work that the seller's disclosure form conveniently left blank. The SDCI's online portal, called the Seattle Services Portal, lets you search any address and pull a full history of building permits, code complaints, and inspection records.

Here's the short answer: go to seattle.gov/sdci, navigate to the Seattle Services Portal, search by address, and review every open or closed permit attached to that property. Pay special attention to permits that were issued but never finaled — that means the work was started, possibly completed, but never officially signed off by an inspector. In a market where the median Seattle home price sits above $800,000, discovering a $40,000 unpermitted basement conversion after closing is not a surprise you want to absorb.

## What Is the SDCI and Why Does It Matter to Buyers?

The Seattle Department of Construction and Inspections is the city agency that oversees all building permits, land use approvals, and code enforcement within Seattle city limits. Every time a homeowner legally adds a bedroom, replaces a furnace, builds a deck, or rewires a panel, they are supposed to pull a permit through SDCI. Those permits create a paper trail that becomes part of the property's official record — permanently attached to the address, not the owner. That means every open issue from the previous owner becomes your problem the moment you sign the deed.

## How to Run an SDCI Permit Lookup Step by Step

1. Go to seattle.gov and navigate to the Seattle Services Portal under the SDCI section.
2. Select 'Permit and Project Search' from the main menu.
3. Enter the property address exactly as it appears in the county records — use the King County Assessor site to confirm the official format if needed.
4. Review the full list of permits returned. Filter by permit type: Building, Electrical, Mechanical, and Plumbing each appear separately.
5. Click into each permit and check the 'Status' field. You want to see 'Final' or 'Completed.' Anything showing 'Issued,' 'In Review,' or 'On Hold' is a flag.
6. Cross-reference permit dates against the home's listed renovation history. A kitchen remodel the seller brags about but with no corresponding permit on record is a serious red flag.
7. Note any Code Compliance cases attached to the address — these are citizen or inspector complaints that trigger enforcement action.

> **A permit that was issued but never finaled is not a closed permit. It means the city never verified the work was done correctly — and that liability transfers to you at closing.**

## The Most Common Permit Red Flags in Seattle Homes

- ADU or DADU conversions with no electrical or mechanical permits — extremely common in Seattle's basement and backyard cottage market.
- Deck additions built without structural permits, especially on hillside lots in neighborhoods like Beacon Hill, Capitol Hill, or Magnolia where grade changes create real load issues.
- Panel upgrades from 100-amp to 200-amp service with no electrical permit — this is one of the most frequently skipped permits in older Craftsman homes.
- Roof replacements with no permit pulled — Seattle requires permits for full roof replacements, and an unverified roof can void your homeowner's insurance claim later.
- Open permits from prior owners dating back 10 or more years that were never resolved — the city can require you, as the new owner, to bring the work up to current code.
- Code Compliance cases marked as 'Active' — these can trigger mandatory inspections or stop-work orders that delay your ability to refinance or sell in the future.

## What Unpermitted Work Actually Costs Seattle Buyers

The financial hit from unpermitted work is rarely just the permit fee. In Seattle, retroactive permit fees for unauthorized construction can run from a few hundred dollars for a simple deck to over $5,000 for an ADU — before you factor in the cost of bringing the work up to current code. If an inspector determines that the unpermitted work was done incorrectly, you may be required to open walls, upgrade electrical panels, or even demolish and rebuild. One commonly cited scenario in Seattle's hillside neighborhoods involves unpermitted retaining walls that fail city structural review — remediation costs in those cases routinely exceed $30,000. Meanwhile, lenders increasingly flag unpermitted square footage during appraisal, which can kill your financing or reduce the appraised value on a property you're already under contract on.

## SDCI Permit Data Has Limits — Here's What It Won't Tell You

The Seattle Services Portal is a powerful starting point, but it has blind spots every buyer should understand. First, permits issued by King County rather than the City of Seattle will not appear in the SDCI system — properties that were annexed into the city after construction may have gaps in their records. Second, the SDCI portal does not show you deed restrictions, easements, or HOA rules that may prohibit the very ADU or renovation the seller claims is fully legal. Third, older permits from before the mid-1990s are sometimes only partially digitized, meaning your search result may show 'no permits found' on a 1950s Craftsman that has had decades of work done. A clean SDCI result is encouraging, but it is not a green light on its own. You still need a licensed home inspector and, for high-value properties, a structural engineer.
