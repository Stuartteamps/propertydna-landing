## Why Luxury Coastal Insurance Is in a Structural Crisis — Not a Cycle

Industry analysts and buyers alike spent most of the 2010s treating coastal insurance premium spikes as cyclical — hurricanes hit, rates rise, markets stabilize. That framework is no longer accurate. What is happening now is structural. Reinsurers — the companies that insure insurance companies — have been quietly withdrawing capacity from U.S. coastal exposure since 2022, forcing primary carriers to either hike rates dramatically or leave markets altogether. Farmers, Bankers Insurance, and Lexington Insurance are among the names that have reduced or eliminated coastal homeowners programs in Florida alone. In California, State Farm and Allstate announced non-renewal campaigns affecting hundreds of thousands of policies before 2024. When the big admitted carriers leave, luxury buyers are routed into the surplus-lines market or state-run insurers of last resort — and both options come with significant trade-offs.

> **When your insurance carrier exits, your mortgage lender doesn't exit with them. You are still required to carry coverage — at whatever price the remaining market charges.**

## What Luxury Coastal Premiums Actually Look Like in 2026

Ballpark figures matter here, even if your specific property will vary. On a $3 million to $5 million oceanfront home in a FEMA Special Flood Hazard Area — designated AE or VE zone — buyers should now budget between $30,000 and $75,000 per year in combined homeowners and flood insurance premiums when using surplus-lines carriers. In VE zones specifically, which carry the highest wave-action risk designation, some policies now require separate named-storm deductibles of 5% to 10% of insured value. On a $4 million home, that is a $200,000 to $400,000 out-of-pocket exposure before your policy pays a single dollar on a hurricane claim. FEMA's National Flood Insurance Program caps coverage at $250,000 for structure and $100,000 for contents — nowhere near adequate for a luxury property — meaning private flood coverage is mandatory, and private flood markets are contracting. This is not hypothetical risk. It is the current market.

- FEMA VE zones: highest coastal flood risk designation; expect the steepest premiums and most restrictive policy terms
- Surplus-lines carriers: not regulated for rate in most states, meaning premiums can change dramatically at renewal with little notice
- Named-storm deductibles: often 2%-10% of insured value, separate from your standard deductible — read the policy declarations page carefully
- Replacement cost vs. ACV: many coastal policies are shifting toward actual cash value payouts, which factor in depreciation and can leave you severely underinsured after a major loss
- Loss of use coverage: on high-end coastal homes that may take 18-24 months to rebuild after a hurricane, this benefit cap matters enormously

Here is the part of the conversation that almost no luxury real estate agent will volunteer: carrier exits do not just affect your insurance cost today — they affect your ability to sell the property in five years. If the only available coverage for your oceanfront address comes from a surplus-lines market with a $50,000 deductible and a 10% named-storm clause, that becomes your buyer's problem at resale. Lenders are already beginning to factor insurability into underwriting decisions. Conventional jumbo loan programs from some banks now require borrowers to document that admitted-carrier coverage is available before approving financing on coastal properties in certain counties. As that trend accelerates, properties in the most exposed zones could face a dual headwind: rising carrying costs and a contracting buyer pool. Luxury does not make you immune to that math.

## Due Diligence Steps Every Luxury Coastal Buyer Should Take in 2026

1. Get an insurance quote before making an offer — not during escrow. Ask your broker to canvas at least three carriers including surplus-lines options, so you have real numbers before you are emotionally committed to the purchase.
2. Pull the FEMA flood map designation for the specific parcel. Know whether you are in an AE, VE, or X zone. VE zone properties face wave-action risk in addition to flooding — insurers treat these as materially different risks.
3. Request the seller's current policy declarations page and the last three years of loss run reports. Prior claims — even minor water claims — can trigger non-renewal or exclusions with a new carrier.
4. Investigate the property's claims history and permit history independently. Unpermitted renovations — common in older coastal luxury homes — can void coverage or create gaps in insured value.
5. Ask your agent specifically: have any carriers non-renewed policies on this address or within this neighborhood in the last 24 months? That question makes a lot of agents uncomfortable, which tells you something.

## Florida vs. California: Two Markets, Same Crisis, Different Details

Florida and California are the two largest luxury coastal markets in the country and both are deep in insurance crisis — but for slightly different reasons. In Florida, the primary driver is hurricane and flood risk, compounded by an unusually high rate of litigation against insurers that drove many carriers out of the state before the weather risk alone would have. Citizens Property Insurance, Florida's insurer of last resort, has been actively trying to reduce its exposure and has raised rates repeatedly. For luxury buyers, Citizens is often not available above a certain coverage threshold anyway, pushing high-value purchasers directly into the surplus-lines market. In California, the crisis is wildfire-driven. Luxury hillside and coastal communities in Los Angeles, Marin, and Santa Barbara counties have seen admitted-carrier non-renewals at scale, and the FAIR Plan — California's last-resort insurer — was never designed to cover $5 million homes. In both states, the regulatory response has lagged significantly behind market reality. Buyers cannot afford to wait for Sacramento or Tallahassee to fix this before they close.

If your due diligence reveals that a luxury coastal property carries materially higher insurance costs than the seller has disclosed — or than comparable inland properties at the same price point — that is a negotiating lever, not just a red flag.
