# California Wildfire Risk Overlay for Home Buyers 2026: What the Maps Aren't Telling You

If you are buying a home in California in 2026, a wildfire risk overlay is not a nice-to-have — it is the difference between owning a property you can insure and finance, and one you cannot. California's official Fire Hazard Severity Zone maps, maintained by CAL FIRE, designate roughly 2.7 million parcels statewide as Moderate, High, or Very High risk. But those maps were last comprehensively updated using data that is already years old, and they do not account for fuel load changes, defensible space conditions, evacuation route capacity, or the parcel-level details that actually determine your financial exposure.

The short answer: always layer multiple data sources — the official FHSZ designation, a private risk score like the ones embedded in the PropertyDNA report, insurance availability data, and permit history for the structure itself — before you make an offer. A seller's disclosure form that says 'High Fire Hazard Zone' is legally required, but it tells you almost nothing about whether you can get homeowner's insurance at a price that keeps your monthly payment in budget. We are going to walk you through exactly what each layer of data means and what to do with it.

## What Is a Wildfire Risk Overlay and Why Do Buyers Need One

A wildfire risk overlay is a geographic data layer — think of it as a transparent sheet placed on top of a property map — that color-codes parcels by their estimated probability of experiencing wildfire damage. The official version in California is the CAL FIRE Fire Hazard Severity Zone designation, which classifies land in State Responsibility Areas into Moderate, High, and Very High tiers. Local Responsibility Areas, which include most incorporated cities, have their own mapping obligations under Assembly Bill 38 and related legislation. The problem is that the official maps are a blunt instrument. They were designed for land-use planning and fire department resource allocation, not for evaluating whether a specific three-bedroom house on a specific lot is a good purchase in 2026.

Private analytics firms now produce parcel-level risk scores that incorporate satellite-derived vegetation data, historical fire perimeter records going back decades, topographic slope and aspect, and even the fire-resistance rating of building materials used in nearby structures. Scores like these are what insurers are actually using to price — or deny — coverage. When an underwriter from a major carrier declines to write a policy on a home in the Sierra Nevada foothills, they are not looking at a CAL FIRE color band. They are looking at a proprietary algorithm that weights dozens of variables. As a buyer, you should be looking at that same level of detail.

## The CAL FIRE FHSZ Map: What It Gets Right and Where It Falls Short

CAL FIRE's Fire Hazard Severity Zone maps do a reasonable job of capturing broad regional risk patterns. If a parcel shows Very High in a State Responsibility Area, that designation carries real legal weight — sellers must disclose it, lenders must factor it into underwriting, and local governments must enforce defensible space requirements. Currently, approximately 67 percent of California's Very High FHSZ parcels are concentrated in just eight counties: Los Angeles, Riverside, San Bernardino, San Diego, El Dorado, Nevada, Placer, and Shasta. If you are shopping in any of those markets, assume that most hillside or canyon-adjacent inventory carries that designation.

Where the official maps fail buyers is at the edges. A property sitting just outside a Very High zone boundary is not necessarily safer than one sitting just inside it. Wind corridors, spot fire behavior, and ember cast can ignite structures a mile from the fire's leading edge. The 2018 Camp Fire destroyed more than 18,800 structures in Paradise, a city that had large sections zoned Moderate risk. The map said moderate. The fire said otherwise.

> **The official zone map tells you what regulators thought in a previous update cycle. A parcel-level risk overlay tells you what the terrain, vegetation, and burn history say today.**

## California's Insurance Crisis and What It Means for Your Purchase in 2026

Since 2019, more than a dozen major insurers — including State Farm, Allstate, and Farmers — have paused or restricted new homeowner's policies in high-risk California ZIP codes. The California FAIR Plan, the state's insurer of last resort, saw its policy count climb past 350,000 as of early 2025, a number that has roughly tripled in four years. FAIR Plan coverage caps out at $3 million for residential property and does not include standard liability protections, meaning buyers often need a separate 'Difference in Conditions' policy layered on top — a combination that can easily run $8,000 to $18,000 annually on a $900,000 foothill property.

Before you remove an insurance contingency on any California property that touches a wildfire risk zone, you need a binder — not a quote, a binder — from an insurer. Your lender will require continuous coverage, and if you close without solving the insurance question first, you can find yourself stuck paying FAIR Plan premiums that blow your debt-to-income ratio. Several buyers in Marin and Santa Barbara counties have had closings collapse post-contract specifically because insurance became unavailable or unaffordable during escrow. Do not let this surprise you.

## The Permit and Hardening History You Must Pull Before You Buy

California law now requires sellers in High and Very High FHSZs to disclose specific home-hardening features as part of the AB 38 disclosure package: roof material, vents, deck material, and window glazing. But disclosure is not the same as verification. A seller checking 'Class A roof material' on a form does not mean a licensed contractor installed it, that it was permitted, or that it has not been partially replaced with non-compliant materials over the years. You want the permit history for the property, which you can pull from the local building department, and you want to cross-reference it against the stated improvements.

- Class A roof covering (composite, tile, or metal) reduces ember ignition risk significantly versus wood shake roofs, which are now banned in high-risk zones but still exist on older homes
- Enclosed eaves and ember-resistant vents are among the highest-impact structural upgrades — an unprotected vent is an open door for a fire brand
- Dual-pane or tempered glass windows resist radiant heat longer than single-pane glass, giving occupants additional evacuation time
- A 0-to-5-foot non-combustible zone immediately around the structure is required by law in FHSZs and is inspectable — ask for the most recent defensible space inspection report
- Permits for any deck rebuild or addition should specify fire-resistant decking material; composite and ignition-resistant treated wood are acceptable, bare pine is not

## How to Read a Parcel-Level Wildfire Risk Score Like a Pro

Parcel-level risk scores typically run on a scale of 1 to 100, with higher numbers representing greater risk, though some providers use letter grades or color bands. The key variables driving these scores include flame length probability (how tall and intense a fire at that location would likely be), exposure probability (the statistical likelihood of fire reaching the parcel within a 30-year period), and consequence factors like structure density and egress limitations. A score of 75 or above on most commercial models should prompt serious due diligence — not necessarily a walk-away, but definitely a conversation with a fire safety engineer and an insurance broker who specializes in high-risk California properties.

What separates a good overlay tool from a bad one is data recency. Vegetation data derived from Landsat or Sentinel-2 satellite imagery can be updated seasonally. A tool using imagery from 2021 in a drought-affected region is not showing you current fuel load — and fuel load is one of the strongest predictors of fire behavior at the parcel level.
