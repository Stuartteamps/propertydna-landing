# Why Your CMA Only Shows 3 Comps When There Are 17 Nearby

When your agent hands you a Comparative Market Analysis, it often contains three, maybe four, comparable sales. Tidy. Confident. Conclusive. But in most active suburban markets, a half-mile radius around almost any listing will surface 12 to 20 closed sales in the past six months that technically qualify as comps. The gap between what you are shown and what exists is not accidental. It is the single most consequential data gap in a residential real estate transaction — and it almost always moves the price in the direction that benefits whoever prepared the report.

The short answer: agents filter comps. They adjust the search radius, the date range, the square footage band, and the condition criteria until the surviving sales support the conclusion they need — a high enough price to list, or a low enough price to justify an offer. That is not necessarily fraud. It is, however, a structural conflict of interest baked into a document that looks like objective analysis. Understanding how to find the other 14 comps — and what they are telling you — is the difference between paying fair market value and overpaying by $30,000 or more.

## How a CMA Is Actually Built (and Who It Is Built For)

A Comparative Market Analysis is a document agents produce inside their MLS platform by pulling closed sales, filtering them, and adjusting values to account for differences between the subject property and each comp. There is no licensing requirement to prepare one, no standardized methodology, and no independent audit. The agent sets every filter manually. The MLS does not flag when a search radius is shrunk from one mile to 0.3 miles to eliminate a cluster of lower-priced sales two blocks away. It does not alert anyone when the date range is tightened from 12 months to 90 days to exclude a softer pricing period. The output looks authoritative — stamped with brokerage branding, printed on heavy paper — but it reflects the judgment calls of someone whose compensation depends on the transaction closing.

> **A CMA is not an appraisal. It is a marketing document. The question is always: who was it marketed to?**

## The 5 Filters Agents Use to Shrink Your Comp Pool

- Search radius reduction: Shrinking from 1 mile to 0.4 miles can eliminate 60 to 70 percent of available sales in dense suburban markets, conveniently excluding lower-priced comps in adjacent neighborhoods.
- Date range compression: Using a 90-day lookback instead of 6 or 12 months removes sales from softer market periods. In a market that peaked 8 months ago, this alone can inflate the apparent price by 5 to 8 percent.
- Square footage bands tightened selectively: Expanding the size range upward to include larger homes pads the average price per square foot. Tightening it downward excludes relevant smaller homes that sold for less.
- Condition and upgrade exclusions: Labeling lower-priced sales as distressed or in poor condition removes them from the pool — even when the subject property has similar deferred maintenance.
- Off-MLS and investor sales excluded: Cash sales to investors, off-market transactions, and new construction closings often transact at lower price points and are routinely omitted from agent-prepared CMAs.

## What the Hidden Comps Usually Reveal

In most cases, the sales that are filtered out of a seller's CMA sold for less than the subject property's asking price. That is precisely why they were filtered. A study of MLS data patterns in competitive metros found that when buyers independently pulled all qualifying comps within a standard one-mile radius and 6-month window, the median adjusted price came in 4.3 percent lower than the price suggested by the agent's curated three-comp CMA. On a $700,000 home, that is $30,100 left on the table before you even begin negotiating. The hidden comps also frequently reveal neighborhood price ceilings — sales that demonstrate what buyers in a given area are actually willing to pay regardless of how a property is staged and photographed.

## Buyer's CMA vs. Seller's CMA: They Are Not the Same Document

A listing agent prepares a CMA to help a seller set a list price — which means the incentive is to present the strongest possible comps to justify a high number and win the listing. A buyer's agent prepares a CMA to help you make an offer — which should mean presenting a conservative picture of what the home is worth. But buyer's agents also earn a percentage of the final purchase price, which means their incentive silently tilts toward a higher closing number, not a lower one. This is the structural conflict that almost no one in the transaction has an incentive to mention. When you see three comps instead of seventeen, ask your agent to pull the full unfiltered set. If they cannot or will not do that, you need a second source.

> **Your agent earns more when you pay more. That is not cynicism — it is math. Run the full comp set yourself.**

## How to Find All the Comps Your Agent Did Not Show You

1. Request the raw MLS pull, not the formatted report. Ask your agent to email you the unfiltered search results showing every closed sale within 1 mile in the last 6 to 12 months, sorted by price per square foot — not adjusted sale price.
2. Check county deed records directly. Every sale is a public record. Your county property appraiser or recorder's website will show every arm's-length transaction in any geographic area, including cash sales that never hit the MLS.
3. Use an independent platform. Tools that pull from both MLS feeds and public records without a commission incentive will surface sales your agent's CMA omitted. PropertyDNA's free reports pull from multiple data sources and show you the full sold landscape around any address — not a curated subset.
4. Look at active listings that failed. Expired listings and withdrawn listings tell you where the ceiling is. A property that sat at $785,000 for 90 days and expired unsold is a comp your agent will never volunteer.
5. Pay attention to the price per square foot range, not just individual sale prices. If 14 of 17 nearby sales closed between $310 and $335 per square foot and the subject property is priced at $372 per square foot, that spread is your negotiating foundation.

## When a Thin Comp Pool Is Legitimate (and When It Is Not)

To be fair, there are properties where a small comp pool is genuinely defensible. Rural parcels with no nearby comparables, architecturally unique homes, or properties with acreage in markets where most homes sit on quarter-acre lots can legitimately produce only two or three meaningful comps. In those cases, appraisers and agents expand search radius, loosen time frames, and make larger manual adjustments — and they disclose that they are doing so. The red flag is not a short comp list. The red flag is a short comp list with no explanation of what was excluded and why. If your CMA shows three comps and does not explain why the other fourteen nearby sales were excluded, that omission is doing work for someone — and it is probably not doing work for you.

The three comps you are shown are a starting position, not a conclusion. Before you write any offer, you should know every qualifying sale in the relevant radius, the price per square foot trend over the last 12 months, whether the market is absorbing inventory faster or slower than six months ago, and what the failed listings at higher price points look like.
